## Identity

The trial of seven is a variant form of trial by combat brought to Westeros during the coming of the Andals, rooted in the Faith of the Seven. In its canonical form, both the accused and the accuser each name seven champions (for a total of fourteen combatants), reflecting the sacred number of the Faith — the seven aspects of the divine. Because it was originally a religious ceremony, it carries special weight within the Faith of the Seven. The trial ends when one side has lost all seven of its champions.

Two historically attested trials of seven are prominent in the narrative. The first, in 42 AC, was the trial held at King's Landing between King Maegor I Targaryen (who claimed the Iron Throne against the objections of the Faith Militant) and seven Warrior's Sons led by Ser Damon Morrigen, their Grand Captain. Maegor was the sole survivor of the battle and wielded Blackfyre throughout; the seven Warrior's Sons who faced him included Aegon Ambrose, Dickon Flowers (the Bastard of Beesbury), Harys Horpe, and others. The second, in 209 AC at the Tourney at Ashford Meadow, was the first trial of seven in over a century: it arose when Ser Duncan the Tall struck Prince Aerion Targaryen to protect a puppet girl, and Aerion demanded a trial of seven. Duncan gathered six champions (including Prince Baelor Targaryen "Breakspear" as his seventh, and Humfrey Beesbury among others); Aerion's side included three Kingsguard knights (Roland Crakehall among them) and Prince Daeron Targaryen. Prince Baelor was accidentally killed by his own brother Maekar during the combat. A formal trial of seven is considered rare enough that the Ashford trial is described as the first in over a hundred years.
