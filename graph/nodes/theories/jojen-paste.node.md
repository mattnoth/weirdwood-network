## Claim

The theory holds that the white weirwood paste Lord Brynden ("Bloodraven") and the
children of the forest feed Bran Stark in his cave beneath the Wall — described as
"thick and heavy, with dark red veins running through it" — is mixed with Jojen
Reed's blood or body, and that Jojen is killed, likely as a blood sacrifice
orchestrated by Bloodraven and/or the children of the forest, sometime during Bran's
stay in the cave. If true, Bran unknowingly commits an act of cannibalism against his
friend at the exact moment his greenseer powers are "awakened." The theory is
explicitly circumstantial: it has no single confirming line of text, and it applies
only to the book continuity — the show kills Jojen by conventional means (stabbed,
then mercy-killed, then the cave collapsed by a child of the forest) with no
paste/cannibalism element at all, so the theory cannot be checked against the show's
resolution of Jojen's fate.

## Evidence For

**The paste's blood-like description.** The bowl Leaf hands Bran is explicitly
described in blood-adjacent terms, with the text itself supplying (and slightly
undercutting) the natural, non-sinister explanation in the same breath:

> The red veins were only weirwood sap, he supposed, but in the torchlight they
> looked remarkably like blood.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:155 (edge J1, node weirwood-paste)

The paste is also described moments earlier as "thick and heavy, with dark red veins
running through it" (adwd-bran-03.md:149) when Leaf first hands Bran the bowl, and
its taste afterward is a startling mix of savory and personal-sentimental notes —
"honey, of new-fallen snow, of pepper and cinnamon and the last kiss his mother ever
gave him" (adwd-bran-03.md:163) — the paste is never chemically identified beyond
"weirwood sap," and neither passage forecloses the theory's inference, but neither
confirms it either.

**Jojen's own resignation to an unstated fate.** In the same chapter, Jojen frames his
role in almost funereal terms, immediately after explaining that greenseeing is a
gift the gods gave to only a few:

> My task was to get you here. My part in this is done.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:35 (edge J2, node jojen-reed)

Meera independently corroborates that Jojen has stopped resisting whatever he has
foreseen:

> He will not even try and fight his fate. He says the greendreams do not lie.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:125 (edge J3, node meera-reed)

Jojen's mood visibly deteriorates across the same stretch of chapter — he "grew ever
more sullen and solitary, to his sister's distress" (edge J4, node jojen-reed,
adwd-bran-03.md:123), and is elsewhere described (food and rest notwithstanding) as
"sadder now, sullen, with a weary, haunted look about the eyes"
(adwd-bran-03.md:29) — behavioral color the theory reads as a person who knows
something bad is coming for him specifically.

An earlier passage, from a different book entirely, is read by the theory as
foreshadowing planted well before the cave chapters. Bran, recalling how unnervingly
accurate Jojen's greendreams are, thinks:

> Except he dreamed me dead, and I'm not. Only he was, in a way.
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-bran-01.md:117 (edge J5, node bran-stark)

The referent of the second "he" is genuinely ambiguous in the prose — it could mean
Jojen himself (the theory's reading) or a metaphorical "death" of Bran's own former
self; the theory leans on the Jojen reading, but the text does not disambiguate it.

**Immediate disappearance and narrative timing.** Right after Bran eats the paste and
has his vision, Jojen and Meera are conspicuously absent, and this is in fact the last
we see of Bran's POV in the published books:

> He had hoped that Meera and Jojen would be there, so he could tell them what he had
> seen, but their snug alcove in the rock was cold and empty.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:205 (edge J8, node jojen-reed)

**The same-chapter human sacrifice vision.** Later in the identical chapter, Bran's
final vision (this time channeled through the weirwood roots rather than an animal or
Hodor) shows an ancient sacrifice to a heart tree, and Bran — as the observing
greenseer — tastes real blood from it:

> but as his life flowed out of him in a red tide, Brandon Stark could taste the
> blood.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:223 (edge J7, node bran-stark)

The theory reads this vision's placement directly after the paste-eating scene, in the
same chapter, as a deliberate authorial juxtaposition of "old gods" human sacrifice
with what Bran has just eaten — though the vision itself depicts a different
sacrifice, of a different captive, in a different (unstated) era, not Jojen's death on
page.

**Recurring motif: unknowing/betrayed cannibalism elsewhere in Bran's own arc.** The
theory situates the paste inside a pattern it traces through Bran's chapters
specifically. At the Nightfort, Bran recalls Old Nan's telling of the Rat Cook, whose
curse for cannibalism-adjacent treachery against a guest is one of the books' most
direct human-cooked-in-a-pie images:

> Afterward the gods transformed the cook into a monstrous white rat who could only
> eat his own young. He had roamed the Nightfort ever since, devouring his children,
> but still his hunger was not sated.
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-bran-04.md:129 (edge J9, node rat-cook)

The same cave chapter's everyday meals carry a lesser echo of the same
uncertain-meat motif: "almost every day they ate blood stew... Jojen thought it might
be squirrel meat, and Meera said that it was rat" (adwd-bran-03.md:89) — food of
unconfirmed origin, eaten without complaint, is already established as normal in this
setting before the paste scene arrives.

**Blood magic has a price elsewhere in the series.** The theory leans on a
cross-book pattern that blood magic of real power requires a costly, specific
ingredient rather than a generic one. Mirri Maz Duur tells Daenerys plainly that
"[b]y itself, the blood is nothing... Do you think bloodmagic is a game for
children?" (agot-daenerys-10.md:13) before the ritual that costs her a horse and her
unborn son; Melisandre similarly insists a working requires a particular pedigree of
blood — "Your brother's blood... A king's blood. Only a king's blood can wake the
stone dragon." (asos-davos-04.md:267). Neither passage mentions Bran, Jojen, or
weirwood paste; they are cited as the series' own established rule that powerful
blood magic is choosy about its blood, which the theory extends (without textual
confirmation) to explain why Jojen's blood specifically — greendreamer, moss-green
eyes — might be what the paste needs.

## Evidence Against

**The stated, mundane explanation for the paste attributes "blood" to Bran, not an
ingredient.** When Bran asks whether the paste will make him a greenseer, Brynden's
answer roots the "blood" language in Bran's own bloodline/gift, not in anything added
to the bowl:

> "Your blood makes you a greenseer," said Lord Brynden. "This will help awaken your
> gifts and wed you to the trees."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:157 (edge J6, node brynden-rivers,
CONTRADICTS)

This is the text's own on-page account of what makes the ritual work, and it does not
gesture at a sacrificed ingredient at all — it complicates, without disproving, the
specific inferential leap that the paste's red veins are Jojen's blood (Brynden is
established elsewhere in the chapter as withholding information from Bran, so the
theory does not treat this as dispositive).

**The video's own hedge.** Alt Shift X's own stated verdict, going into the
argument, is that there is "no direct textual confirmation either way" — the video
argues the accumulated circumstantial evidence makes the reading "very plausible,
even likely," not proven. See Status Notes.

## Ungrounded material (outside the corpus)

- **show** — Game of Thrones (Season 4) kills Jojen Reed conventionally: stabbed by
  wights, then mercy-killed by Meera, with a child of the forest detonating the cave
  to cover their escape. No paste, no cannibalism angle, no ambiguity about his death
  at all. This is a show-only divergence; the theory only applies to book continuity,
  and the show's resolution neither confirms nor denies it.
- **book-inference (not independently grounded)** — Several beats the source video
  cites could not be located verbatim in the 5-book corpus search and are omitted
  from the edges above: that Coldhands feeds Bran the flesh of dead Night's Watchmen
  and calls it "pork" on the journey north; that Bran eats Coldhands' elk and
  specifically calls it a "friend"; that Stannis's starving soldiers eat their own
  dead; that Wyman Manderly's Frey pies are textually confirmed as cannibalism (the
  wiki/fandom treats this as settled, but the search did not locate an on-page
  confirming line); and that Euron Greyjoy's blood sacrifices for his fleet, and
  Skagosi human sacrifice to weirwoods, are stated outright rather than implied.
  These remain plausible ASOIAF beats but are not sourced to a specific passage here.
- **literary-parallel (not an edge)** — The video's broader "disillusionment arc"
  argument — that Bran's realization Bloodraven is a monster parallels Sansa's
  realization that Cersei is not the fairy-tale queen she imagined — could not be
  cleanly grounded: the specific quote the search matched for Sansa's supposed
  verdict on Cersei ("vile, scheming, evil bitch," affc-cersei-10.md:173) is
  actually **Kevan Lannister** speaking to Cersei, not a Sansa POV line, and is a
  false-positive keyword match. The parallel-arc argument itself — plus the
  observation that Bran has already used Hodor's body against his will
  (adwd-bran-03.md:113) and already eaten while warged into Summer
  (asos-bran-01.md:55, animal predation, not human flesh) — is held out of the
  edges entirely: it is literary-critical pattern-matching about Bran's character
  arc in general, not textual evidence that Jojen specifically is in the paste.
- **atmosphere (cited in prose above only, not edges)** — Bloodraven's monstrous
  physical description ("less a man than some ghastly statue made of twisted wood,
  old bone, and rotted wool," adwd-bran-03.md:115; roots "snaking in and out of his
  withered flesh," adwd-bran-03.md:117), his manipulative mentorship rhetoric
  ("Darkness will make you strong," adwd-bran-03.md:45), the cave's "bones of
  thousands dead" (adwd-bran-03.md:91), and Leaf's unproven claim — "For us, for
  you, for the realms of men" (adwd-bran-03.md:25) — all build a portrait of Bloodraven as an unsettling,
  possibly self-interested figure capable of ordering something dark — but none of
  it names Jojen or the paste, so none of it is minted as an edge to this node.
  Likewise the chapter's opening dark imagery (dead wights, "whispers in the dark,"
  adwd-bran-03.md:11) and the four-times-repeated crescent-moon line
  (adwd-bran-03.md:11, :47 — only 2 of the claimed 4 recurrences were located) set
  mood without asserting anything.

## Status Notes

**ASX verdict:** EhtbVpc8E70 explicitly states there is "no direct textual
confirmation either way," but argues the accumulated circumstantial evidence — the
paste's blood-like description, Jojen's resignation and disappearance, the
same-chapter sacrifice vision, and the series-wide pattern of costly blood magic and
unknowing cannibalism in Bran's own arc — makes the reading "very plausible, even
likely." The video itself frames this as its own hedge, not a claim of proof; `status:
open` reflects that neither the video nor the book text (through *A Dance with
Dragons*, the last published volume as of this writing) resolves the question. No
GRRM interview statement or authorial confirmation/denial was located in the source
material for this cluster; if one surfaces, `status` should move to `show-confirmed`
(unlikely, given the show's divergent resolution) or `jossed`, not left `open`.
