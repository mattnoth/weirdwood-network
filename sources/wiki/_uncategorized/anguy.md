In the television adaptation Game of Thrones, Anguy is portrayed by Philip McGinley.

Appearance and Character[ ]

See also: Images of Anguy

Anguy is skinny with freckles and red hair. He is fairly young.

Anguy carries a longbow and uses arrows tipped with bodkins as well as broadheads. He is highly valued due to his exceptional skill with a bow. The only songs he knows are marcher ballads.

Fern, a girl at Lambswold, fancies Anguy.

Recent Events[ ]

A Game of Thrones[ ]

During the Hand's tourney at King's Landing in celebration of Lord Eddard Stark's ascension as Hand of the King, Anguy wins the archery competition, outshooting Ser Balon Swann and Jalabhar Xho at a hundred paces and earning himself a sum of ten thousand golden dragons. He refuses an offer from Eddard to join the Hand's guard.

Having spent and lost his fortune, Anguy joins Lord Beric Dondarrion when Eddard dispatches him to bring Ser Gregor Clegane to justice.

A Storm of Swords[ ]

Anguy is with the brotherhood without banners party that captures Arya Stark, Gendry, and Hot Pie. He shoots a duck which the outlaws give to Sharna, and then a goose as they continue their travels. Arya, who is unable to draw Anguy's longbow, is told tales of the Dornish Marches by the archer. He offers to make a light bow for her once they get to Riverrun. Anguy uses his arrows to put northmen starving in crow cages out of their misery at Stoney Sept, and he flirts with a freckle-faced wench at the Peach.

The outlaws bring the three captured youths to Beric in the hollow hill. Like most members of the brotherhood, Anguy is now a follower of R'hllor.

Anguy takes part in the battle at the burning septry with the Brave Companions, shooting several of the sellswords. He reveals he squandered his fortune at Chataya's brothel, enjoying lavish excesses with Jayde, Alayaya, and Dancy. Beric orders Anguy to aid in keeping watch for Sandor Clegane. Arya helps Anguy dry horses at the ruins of a Goodbrook village.

A Feast for Crows[ ]

After Beric's final death, a part of the brotherhood, including Anguy, go their separate way from those who chose to follow the resurrected, vengeful Lady Stoneheart.

Behind the Scenes[ ]

George R. R. Martin was probably inspired by his father when he wrote about Anguy spending ten thousand golden dragons on women and drinking as his father won ten thousand dollars by gambling during World War II. He had a good time with the money, spending it all on women, beer, nightclubs, and the track.
