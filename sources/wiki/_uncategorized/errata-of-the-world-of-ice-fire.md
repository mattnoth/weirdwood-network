Background[ ]

In his A Song of Ice and Fire series, George R. R. Martin uses the "unreliable narrator". As such, not all inconsistencies in the books that are mistakes. However, Martin has expressed that accidental mistakes can distract from the planned inconsistencies, making them less effective. As such, the pages indexed here represent a collection of gathered possible and confirmed mistakes and, if possible, their confirmed corrections within Martin's series.

What you will not find on this page are discussions of information from the So Spake Martins which contradicts with more recently released printed material, contradictions found in sample chapters from unreleased books (e.g. The Winds of Winter), or speculations about possible solutions.

Examples of what will not be found on the errata pages include:

According to Osha, "the blue star in the rider's eye" of the constellation the Ice Dragon points north. However, when Bran Stark later recalls Osha's statement, he does so incorrectly: "The blue star in the dragon's eye pointed the way north, as Osha told him once."

Differences in views on certain events. While the "commonly told story about the Battle of the Bells" apparently states that Robert I Baratheon and Jon Connington did not come face to face during the battle (as stated by Maester Yandel ["Rightly famed is Robert's grand victory at Stoney Sept, also called the Battle of the Bells, where he slew the famous Ser Myles Mooton—once Prince Rhaegar's squire—and five men besides, and might well have killed the new Hand, Lord Connington, had the battle brought them together."] and Harwin of Winterfell ["Robert came out of hiding to join the fight when the bells began to ring. He slew six men that day, they say. One was Myles Mooton, a famous knight who'd been Prince Rhaegar's squire. He would have slain the Hand too, but the battle never brought them together."]), Connington himself recalls it rather differently ("Bells and battle followed, and Robert emerged from his brothel with a blade in hand, and almost slew Jon on the steps of the old sept that gave the town its name.").

The World of Ice & Fire[ ]

The Reign of the Dragons: The Conquest[ ]

The original text suggests Archmaester Gyldayn lived in King Aegon V's time and his work on history was sent to Archmaester Gerold, then lost in time, with only fragments remaining. However, George R. R. Martin later decided to let Gyldayn live into Robert I Baratheon's reign, so his history book could cover the entire Targaryen Dynasty. Hence in latest prints, Gyldayn shared his uncompleted work with Archmaester Perestan, who then informed Yandel. And Yandel persuaded Gyldayn to permit the use of  passages from Gyldayn's work in Yandel's book.

It says Aegon marched northeast, from Aegonfort to the Gods Eye and Harrenhal. However, the direction should be "northwest". The error is fixed in Fire & Blood. 

It says "Thrice Argilac the Arrogant led his knights against the Baratheon positions...and the third and final charge of the Storm King and his knights broke through the Baratheon center...only to come upon Queen Rhaenys and Meraxes." It should be the fourth charge that broke through the Baratheon center. The error is fixed in Fire & Blood. 

The Targaryen Kings[ ]

Aegon I

It is mentioned that, as a result of the First Dornish War, there were "would-be assassins in King’s Landing and the Red Keep itself". However, the Red Keep was built decades after the First Dornish War ended.

A first-run print of The World of Ice & Fire includes a mention of Prince Aenys Targaryen as the "Prince of Dragonstone" during Aegon I's reign in The Targaryen Kings: Aegon I, but this was confirmed to be a first print error which has been corrected in later versions.

Aenys I

It is said King Aegon I Targaryen "passed at the age of four-and-sixty" in the year 37 AC. However, since he was born in the year 27 BC and had conquered the Seven Kingdoms at the age of twenty-seven, in 1 AC, after a war of two years (2 BC-1 AC), Aegon was actually sixty-three years old when he died in 37 AC.

King Aenys I Targaryen was said to be "crowned with great ceremony in the Red Keep in the midst of its construction, donning an ornate golden crown rather than his father’s circlet of Valyrian steel." However, in Fire & Blood it is described how he received his ornate golden crown in Oldtown.

Alyssa Velaryon's mother is said to have been a Targaryen. However, in Fire & Blood she is identified as Lady Alarra Massey.

Jonos Arryn is said to have "died by the noose, at Maegor's hand", but as of Fire & Blood, Jonos died by being thrown through the Moon Door by his own men.

Aenys I Targaryen is stated to be attacked by the Poor Fellows "in the castle", which implied the half-completed Red Keep. However, in The Sons of the Dragon and Fire & Blood he is described to have lived in a manse atop Visenya's Hill by that time.

Maegor I

Princess Rhaena Targaryen, the daughter of King Aenys I Targaryen and Queen Alyssa Velaryon, was born in 23 AC. In the chapter concerning Maegor I's reign, the section discussing the possible betrothal between Rhaena and Maegor ("[Ceryse Hightower] was advanced by her uncle, the High Septon, after he protested the betrothal of the thirteen-year-old Prince Maegor to Maegor's newborn niece, Princess Rhaena. Ceryse and Maegor were married in 25 AC.") implies that Maegor was thirteen years old at the time of the suggestion, placing the proposed betrothal in 25 AC. However, the wording was formulated as such as to get Maegor's age at his marriage in 25 AC across, not his age at the suggestion of the betrothal, which has been established to have occurred in 23 AC shortly after Maegor had turned eleven.

Ceryse Hightower's father was stated as Martyn Hightower, which is corrected to Manfred Hightower in Fire & Blood.

The chapter on the reign of Maegor I incorrectly places the mysterious death of the High Septon (following Maegor's threat of burning Oldtown) in 44 AC. However, The Sons of the Dragon places this event in 43 AC. This date is the correct one; Seemingly, The World of Ice & Fire accidentally mixed the mysterious death of this High Septon when Maegor threatens Oldtown (in 43 AC) with the natural death of his successor a year later.

It is stated that Septon Moon and Ser Joffrey Doggett led the Poor Fellows against King Maegor I. However, this was changed for Fire & Blood, where Ser Joffrey Doggett led the Warrior's Sons.

The chapter on the reign of Maegor I incorrectly states that Alys Harroway was the first of his wives to become pregnant in 48 AC. It has been confirmed that the correct date is 44 AC, as is indeed stated in The Sons of the Dragon.

On the eve of Maegor's death, after long discussion, the lords departed "the council chamber", leaving Maegor alone. However, in Fire & Blood the discussion was held in the throne room.

Jaehaerys I

The chapter on the reign of Jaehaerys I Targaryen say "Robar Baratheon" was Lord Protector of the Realm and Hand of the King in the early years. However, the original manuscript used both "Robar" and "Rogar", with the former being chosen for The World of Ice & Fire as it was the name Martin wrote down towards the end of the manuscript. As revealed by Garcia in July 2018, Martin decided he preferred the name "Rogar", which is used in Fire & Blood.

In the chapter on Jaehaerys I's reign, it is stated that Jaehaerys became king at the age of 14 in 48 AC and married his sister once he reached his majority two years later, in 50 AC. It is also stated that the marriage lasted 46 years. However, the marriage only ended at Alysanne's death in either 99 AC or 100 AC, forty-nine or fifty years after the wedding. Regardless, the statement of forty-six years is still correct, as the years of the First and Second Quarrel (both having lasted a year or two), are not counted among their "married years".

The artwork by Marc Simonetti depicting the great tourney of 98 AC, shows King's Landing with the Great Sept of Baelor atop Visenya's Hill, though it would not be built for nearly a century. A grand sept was atop Visenya's Hill during that time, but the white marble, the dome, and the seven crystal towers clearly depict Baelor's Sept.

The text claims Jaehaerys and Septon Barth reconciled with the Faith, by dispatching Septon Barth to Oldtown to speak with the High Septon and to forge a lasting agreement. This is not so in Fire & Blood.

In the chapter discussing the reign of Jaehaerys I, the castle town of Harrentown is misspelled as “Harrenton”.

Princess Rhaenys Targaryen, is described as Prince Aemon Targaryen's eldest daughter in the chapter on Jaehaerys I Targaryen's reign. However, she is his only daughter (and child).

The text implies Gael Targaryen drowned herself with "a growing belly", but in Fire and Blood she drowned herself after stillbirth.

Jaehaerys I and Queen  Alysanne's ashes are said to be buried beneath the Red Keep. However, Fire & Blood revealed their ashes were interred on Dragonstone.

The chapter on Jaehaerys I's reign, Septon Barth is stated to have died in 99 AC. Fire and Blood states on several occasions that Barth died in 98 AC.

The text suggests Alicent Hightower came to King's Landing and became Jaehaerys's companion at fifteen. However, according to Fire & Blood, she came to King's Landing at thirteen and became Jaehaerys's companion at fifteen.

Viserys I

Laenor Velaryon is described as having "the blood of the dragon on both sides", a statement repeated in The Rogue Prince. However, Fire & Blood removed this claim.

Grand Maester Orwyle is described as having amputated King Viserys I Targaryen's fingers in 128 AC in the chapter on Viserys I's reign, whereas The Rogue Prince states this was done by maester Gerardys. Fire & Blood confirmed that Gerardys is correct, although the year in question was changed to 126 AC. The issue is partially fixed in later version of The World of Ice & Fire.

Aegon II

It is stated Stone Hedge was taken during the Battle of the Burning Mill. However, they were two separate battles.

It is stated Septon Barth, who died decades before the Dance of the Dragons, was dubious about the smallfolk's claim that the Cannibal have lurked on Dragonstone even before the coming of House Targaryen. In Fire & Blood Barth is replaced by Eustace, living at the time of the war.

In the chapter on Aegon II Targaryen's reign, Battle of the Gullet occurred in 129 AC. But Fire & Blood established it in 130 AC. The issue is fixed in later version of The World of Ice & Fire.

Bryndon Hightower's name is spelled as "Brynden".

In the chapter on Aegon II 's reign, Willum Royce is changed into Willam Royce in Fire & Blood.

It is mentioned that some of Corlys Velaryon's sworn swords scaled the Red Keep's walls trying to free the Sea Snake, only to be hanged when they were caught. However, according to Fire & Blood, several men loyal to House Velaryon slipped over the walls of King's Landing, intent on making their way back to Driftmark, while Ser Denys Woodwright and Ser Thoron True attempted to cut their way into the dungeons of the Red Keep in order to liberate Lord Corlys.

Baela Targaryen was twice referred to as "princess", which should be "maid" or "lady".

It is mentioned that Helaena Targaryen's death and the subsequent riot of King's Landing began the same day when the Battle Above the Gods Eye was fought, on the twenty-second day of the fifth moon of 130 AC. However, Fire & Blood does not confirm that temporal connection, and The Rise of the Dragon says that the uprising happened days after Daemon Targaryen's death.

Aegon III

It is stated that eleven-year-old King Aegon agreed to make Cregan Stark his Hand. However, Fire & Blood specifies the king was still ten years old at the time.

The section on the regents in the chapter of Aegon III's reign states that Thaddeus Rowan retired in 136 AC and Corwyn Corbray died in 134 AC. However, Fire & Blood sets both events at 135 AC.

The ladies Baela and Rhaena Targaryen, Prince Daemon Targaryen's twin daughters, are both called princess in the chapter on Aegon III Targaryen's reign, while they are referred to as ladies in The Princess and the Queen and elsewhere in The World of Ice & Fire. This has been fixed in later editions of The World of Ice & Fire, where they are correctly called "ladies".

Daenaera Velaryon was claimed to be "the last of a thousand maids who had been presented" at the Maiden's Day Ball. However, in Fire & Blood there were more than a thousand maids at the ball, and "fewer than a dozen maids" behind Daenaera.

Baelor I

The section on the sisters of Baelor I Targaryen in the chapter on Baelor I's reign states that "[...] and that the child conceived that night was by her cousin Aegon - he who later became King Aegon the Unworthy." in reference to the conception of Elaena Targaryen's son Viserys Plumm in 176 AC. However, the statement is incorrect in stating that "her cousin Aegon" would later become King Aegon; Aegon was already king in 176 AC.

The section on the sisters of Baelor I Targaryen in the chapter on Baelor I's reign states that Princess Elaena Targaryen had two children by her second husband, Ronnel Penrose, but the family tree states that Elaena had four children with her Penrose husband.

Michael Manwoody is claimed to "become a trusted servant to King Daeron after Daeron's marriage to Queen Myriah". But Daeron married long before he became the king and Myriah the queen.

Aegon IV

Grand Maester Munkun is Grand Maester both during the regency of King Aegon III Targaryen (131 AC – 136 AC) and at the death of King Baelor I in 171 AC, while in 153 AC, Grand Maester Alford was said to have held the office. It has been confirmed that this is not a mistake, however, though the explanation will only be provided in Fire & Blood.

The chapter on the reign of King Aegon IV Targaryen reign states that Bellegere Otherys was the child of a Braavosi merchant's daughter and an envoy from the Summer Isles, but that is not correct. Instead, she is the daughter of the son of a Sealord of Braavos and a princess from the Summer Isles. This has been corrected in later kindle editions.

Daeron II

Daemon I Blackfyre is said to have married Rohanne of Tyrosh when he was fourteen. However, since Daemon was born in late 170 AC and the marriage took place in early 184 AC, right after the death of Aegon IV Targaryen, he was actually still thirteen at the time of the wedding.

Princess Myriah Martell's name, which was "Myriah" in the main series, was changed into "Mariah" in The World of Ice & Fire, but has been changed back to "Myriah Martell" in Fire & Blood.

In the artwork by Magali Villeneuve, which depicts King Daeron II Targaryen and Prince Maron Martell paying their respects to the statue of Baelor the Blessed, the statue appears to be the wrong Baelor— King Baelor I had long silver-gold hair and a beard, and never bore a sword, whereas Daeron's son Baelor had short dark hair and was a swordsman.

Aerys I

The Second Blackfyre Rebellion is incorrectly stated to have taken place in 211 AC in the chapter on the reign of Aerys I Targaryen. It has been corrected to 212 AC in later editions of The World of Ice & Fire.

Aegon V

The timeline of Duncan the Small's abdication is ambiguous. The World of Ice & Fire first states that he gave up his claim to the throne before Lyonel Baratheon's rebellion, but later states that peace was only restored after his renouncement. According to The World of Ice & Fire co-author Elio García, the first statement is "more correct", while the second can be read as "eliding that Duncan gave up his claim fruitlessly" and noting that "it was the end state of things when peace was restored".

The chapter on Aegon V Targaryen's reign suggests Gyldayn's history ended at the Tragedy at Summerhall. However, George R. R. Martin later decided to let Gyldayn live into Robert I Baratheon's reign, so his history book could cover the entire Targaryen Dynasty. Fire & Blood suggests Gyldayn knew Robert's Rebellion. In latest prints, the author of the fragment recording the Tragedy at Summerhall was changed from Gyldayn to Summerhall's Maester Corso.

Aerys II

When Elia was betrothed to Rhaegar in early 279 AC, she was described as "the delicate young sister of Doran Martell, Prince of Dorne". However, according to Oberyn Martell, Doran's mother, the Princess of Dorne arranged Elia Martell's betrothal. So Doran was not the ruling prince then.

The date of the announcement of the Tourney at Harrenhal is contradictory. The chapter on the reign of Aerys II states it was first announced in 281 AC, while the next chapter, The Year of the False Spring, states it was first announced in 280 AC. In later editions, the date of the announcement was changed to 280 AC, with the phrase in "Aerys II" having been changed to 'shortly before Tywin Lannister's resignation in 281 AC'.

The Fall of the Dragons[ ]

The dates and locations of the Anniversary Tourney of 272 AC and Lannisport tourney of 276 AC are confused across several chapters. In the chapter on the reign of Aerys II Targaryen, the "great Anniversary Tourney of 272 AC" in King's Landing is mentioned, as is the Lannisport tourney, mentioned to have taken place in 276 AC. But in the chapter on The Year of the False Spring, it is incorrectly stated that "the great Lannisport Tourney" took place in 272 AC (the tourney in 272 AC was the Anniversary Tourney in King's Landing). However, it is correct that both tourney's were organized by Tywin.

In the chapter The Year of the False Spring Prince Viserys is mentioned to be seven years old. However, the False Spring takes place in 281 AC, and Viserys was born in 276 AC, meaning that he was five years old, not seven.

The chapter on Robert's Rebellion originally stated that "And after a partial victory at Ashford, which led to Robert's withdrawal, the Stormlands were left open to Lord Tyrell. " This has been changed to "The partial victory at Ashford, which led to Robert's march to the riverlands, had left the Stormlands open to Lord Tyrell." at least in the Bantam kindle ebook version v3.1r4)

The Seven Kingdoms[ ]

The North:

Earlier prints of The World of Ice & Fire state that Lord Ellard Stark was the lord who had been forced to give up the New Gift.  However, it has been confirmed that this is an error, and that the statement would be replaced by "the Starks were glad" in later prints, leaving it unknown for the time being which Stark had been the lord in question. In the latest version, the statement is changed to "Though in these days it is said that Lord Alaric Stark was glad to aid the Night’s Watch with the Gift, and took little convincing, the truth is otherwise. Letters from Lord Stark’s sons to the Citadel, asking the maesters to provide precedents against the forced donation of property, made it plain that the Starks were not eager to do as King Jaehaerys bid."

The Riverlands:

In the first edition, the Riverlands map labels the High Road as the "River Road".

The Riverlands map labels the Inn at the Crossroads south of the Trident, while the books state it lies to the north.

In the first print, in the chapter on House Tully, it is incorrectly stated that "As his enemies rose around him, the Tullys rallied to the banners of Prince Jaehaerys Targaryen, son of the slain Prince Aegon, in the final year of his cruel uncle's reign". Jaehaerys was Aegon's younger brother, not his son. This has been fixed in later prints, changing "son" to "brother".

Benedict I Justman is called "the first of the Andal kings" in the riverlands, but immediately after is identified as the bastard son of a Blackwood and a Bracken, both First Men houses. According to Elio Garcia, co-author of The World of Ice & Fire, "What [George R. R. Martin] must have meant was that [Benedict] was the first king to follow the Seven to unify the riverlands, as the Andal petty kings before him just fought one another over their little petty kingdoms. Will tweak that in future editions". Indeed, later editions have changed the sentence "HOUSE MUDD, of Oldstones (last dynasty of the First Men to rule the riverlands)" present in the original prints into "HOUSE MUDD, of Oldstones (last dynasty to worship the old gods in the riverlands)" in later editions, as Benedict I Justman, being half-Blackwood and half-Bracken, was a First Men. This fix has been incorporated at least in the Bantam kindle ibook version v3.1r4)

Archmaester Perestan's book  A Consideration of History is incorrectly referred to as  A Consideration on History.

It states that Lyonel Strong was the first Lord of Harrenhal from House Strong, which has been changed to Bywin Strong, and future editions of The World of Ice & Fire will be adjusted.

The Riverlands & The Iron Islands:

The chapter on the riverlands section says that House Justman came to power after the Andal invasion. (The first of the Andal kings to bring all the riverlands under his sway was a bastard born of a tryst between two ancient enemies, the Blackwoods and the Brackens).  The line of House Justman was ended by Qhored the Cruel. (Their line was ended when Qhored Hoare, King of the Iron Islands, murdered the sons of King Bernarr II whilst they were held captive in Pyke. Their father did not long survive them, provoked into a hopeless war for vengeance against the ironborn.) After "a hundred years of conflict", House Teague won the rule over the riverlands. However, the chapter on the iron islands section mentions that the Andals arrived on the mainland of Westeros only after the Iron Kings had taken power. The Iron Kings ruled after Qhored, a High King, creating an inconsistency. Therefore, the following changes are made in later version of The World of Ice & Fire.

In The Iron Islands: Driftwood Crowns, the sentence "By his day, the First Men of the green lands had largely abandoned the shores of the Sunset Sea for fear of the reavers." is to be changed into "By his day, the men of the green lands had largely abandoned the shores of the Sunset Sea for fear of the reavers."

In The Iron Islands: Driftwood Crowns, the sentence "And late in the Age of Heroes, another crisis weakened and divided the ironborn further still." Is to be changed into "And then came another crisis weakened and divided the ironborn further still."

In The Iron Islands: The Iron Kings, the sentence "The arrival of the Andals in the Seven Kingdoms only hastened the decline of the iron islands." Is to be changed into "The arrival of the Andals in the Seven Kingdoms centuries before had only hastened the decline of the iron islands."

With these changes, the situation becomes such that there were Andals present in the late driftwood king period, who had slowly begun to have an effect on things to start with but eventually became more and more of a problem for the ironborn. As formulated by Elio Garcia, "The Justmans were a post-Andal house some decades/centuries after the fall of the Mudds, they lasted three centuries, Qhored I Hoare took them out, but the decline of the ironborn that followed was obviously tied to the growing presence of the Andals on the mainland. And then as to Teague, Torrence Teague started a dynasty that eventually lost the throne, but centuries later another Teague had hold of things. No errata for that last, don't think we'll change anything regarding the Teagues in the text."

As follows from the above mentioned changes, the sentence "The kings who followed Qhored played a part in that, yet the men of the green lands were likewise growing stronger. The First Men were building longships of their own." should be changed to "The kings who followed Qhored played a part in that, yet the men of the green lands were likewise growing stronger. They/The rivermen were building longships of their own", as Qhored lived after the Andal invasion.

Two different locations are given for the place where Harwyn Hoare came ashore in the riverlands. The chapter on the riverlands states that "Crossing Ironman's Bay with a hundred longships, Harwyn's force landed forty leagues south of Seagard and marched inland to the Blue Fork, carrying their ships with them on their shoulders in a feat the singers of the isles still celebrate.", while the section on the Iron Islands states "Harwyn assembled a host and led it across the bay on a hundred of his father's longships. Landing unchallenged north of Seagard, they carried their ships overland to the Blue Fork of the Trident, then swept downstream with fire and sword." The Blue Fork is located south of Seagard.

The Vale:

The map in the chapter on the Vale gives a wrong location for Saltpans and Maidenpool, placing them farther east than where them are supposed to be. The map in the chapter on the riverlands is correct.

The Lady Forlorn of Robar II Royce's era is described to be a Valyrian steel sword. It has been confirmed that "Valyrian steel" should have been removed from the description.

The chapter on House Arryn states that House Arryn married into House Targaryen twice. However, it is supposed to be trice (Daella Targaryen to Lord Rodrik Arryn, Aemma Arryn to Viserys I Targaryen, and Alys Arryn to Rhaegel Targaryen).

The Iron Islands:

Pyke is described as located on "half a dozen islets and sea stacks". However, Theon described it as located on "three bare and barren islands and a dozen towering stacks of rock".

It is stated "The Red Kraken himself led the attack that captured Kayce." But in Fire & Blood Joanna Lannister and her westermen were able to rescue Kayce from being captured. It is suggested to modify the statement to "The Red Kraken himself led the attack that nearly captured Kayce" in later prints.

The Westerlands:

The westerlands map places the Golden Tooth too southwest, compared with its location in The Lands of Ice and Fire.

It is stated that "The Lannisters of Lannisport prospered, built great walls around their city to defend it from those (chiefly ironborn) who sought to steal their wealth, and soon became kings", whereas in George R.R. Martin's original draft, the sentence is "The Lannisters of Lannisport prospered, and built great walls around their city to defend it from those who sought to steal their wealth (chiefly ironborn). The Lannisters of Casterly Rock became kings". The latter seems to be correct.

Earlier prints list House Parren as both a family from the westerlands and the Reach. Later editions have removed them from the houses in the westerlands, establishing them as a house from the Reach. Most recent editions, however, have removed them from the Reach and placed them back as a house from the Westerlands.

The chapter on The Westerlands discusses King Tyrion III and his son Gerold II who reigned the westerlands during the Andal Invasion. Earlier prints mention "Loreon IV was better known as Loreon the Lackwit […]. A later monarch, Tyrion II, was known as the Tormentor.", implying that Tyrion II (an Andal king) reigned after Tyrion III (king when the Andals finally arrived). Although no fix has yet been printed, one of the following two corrections should be incorporated: dropping "later", or changing "II" to "IV".

In the chapter on the westerlands, Joffrey Lydden's name is misspelled as "Joffery Lydden".

Tywin Lannister is stated to have been named Hand of the King by King Aerys II Targaryen in 262 AC at the age of twenty on page in the first edition. On page 201, it is stated that Genna Lannister was betrothed in 254 AC, an event where Tywin was known to have been ten years old. The date of the betrothal has been confirmed to have been incorrect, with the "next print" (i.e. fourth print) and ibooks correctly stating 252 AC as the year in which Genna was betrothed.

The chapter on the Westerlands misspells Houses Drox and Brax with an E on the end.

The chapter on the westerlands mentions "Stafford Lannister, the eldest son and heir of Lord Tytos's late brother, Ser Jason." However, Stafford is not Jason's eldest son. Jason's firstborn son was Damon Lannister, born from Jason's first marriage. Stafford was Jason's first son from his second marriage, and his second born son overall. Later editions have changed this to "Stafford Lannister, the eldest son of the late Ser Jason Lannister by his second wife." 

Two statements regarding Lomas Longstrider and Asshai contradict one another. In the chapter on the westerlands it is said that "Lomas Longstrider reports that, even in far Asshai-by-the-Shadow, there were merchants who asked him if it was true that the Lion Lord lived in a palace of solid gold [...]", while the chapter on Asshai states "Who can say? Lomas Longstrider never saw Asshai-by-the-Shadow."

The chapter on the westerlands states "Ellyn Reyne, now Lady Tarbeck, departed Casterly Rock with her husband, never to return, [...]". However, she returned at least once as Genna Lannister mentions Ellyn to have been present at Casterly Rock at the announcement of her own betrothal to Emmon Frey in 252 AC.

The Reach:

The section on Oldtown states that "Some believe His High Holiness was removed by his own brother, Ser Morgan Hightower, commander of the Warrior's Sons in Oldtown […]". However, this is in error, as Morgan was the brother of Lord Martyn Hightower, not of the High Septon.

The section on Oldtown gives the mysterious death of the High Septon (following Maegor's threat of burning Oldtown) in 44 AC. However, The Sons of the Dragon places this event in 43 AC. This date is the correct one; Seemingly, The World of Ice & Fire accidentally mixed the mysterious death of this High Septon when Maegor threatens Oldtown (in 43 AC with the natural death of his successor a year later.

Harlan Tyrell was named Lord Paramount of the Mander by King Aegon I. However, the section of House Tyrell states that "Aegon Targaryen choose to name the Tyrells as Lords of Highgarden, Wardens of the South, and Lords Paramount of the Reach".

The Stormlands:

Ser Laenor Velaryon is referred to twice as "Prince" in The World of Ice & Fire. This is a confirmed error.

The maps released in A Feast for Crows and A Dance with Dragons place Felwood south of the Wendwater, but on the map of the stormlands in The World of Ice & Fire it's located where the river originates. The map from The World of Ice & Fire is in error.

The chapter on House Baratheon calls Aerys the eldest son of King Jaehaerys II. However, Aerys was Jaehaerys's only son.

The chapter states that the Kingsguard Ser Raymont Baratheon saved Aenys I Targaryen from a murder attempt by two Poor Fellows. However, the name of the Kingsguard who saved Aenys is not given in Fire & Blood. It is suggested not to name Raymont explicitly in future editions of The World of Ice & Fire.

Aemond Targaryen was referred to as Lucerys Velaryon's "cousin". It should be "uncle".

In earlier prints, the chapter on the Stormlands describes Alysanne Blackwood as Lord Benjicot Blackwood's sister. However, "Black Aly Blackwood" has been confirmed to have been the aunt of Benjicot Blackwood, not his sister. This has been corrected in later editions.

In the first print, Rhaegar Targaryen is incorrectly stated to have died in 282 AC. This has been corrected in later editions to 283 AC.

Dorne:

The name of the river Greenblood is once misspelled as "Greenwood" in The World of Ice & Fire.

In an instance it is said that "House Martell has guided Dorne for seven hundred years". However, other passages from The World of Ice & Fire and the main series claim that the Second Spice War and the subsequent Dornish unification by Nymeria and House Martell happened a thousand years ago. According to Elio García, the first statement is an editorial mistake: the seven hundred years mentioned are actually the seven centuries of Martell's reign before Aegon's Conquest.

It is stated Lord Fowler seized and burned Nightsong and carried off "its lords and defenders" as hostages. However, according to Fire and Blood, only "Lord Caron and his wife and children" were taken as captives, the garrison were put to the sword.

"Word" is misspelled as "sword" once: not with iron and fire but with soft swords and smiles and a pair of well-considered marriages.

It is stated that "But for the madness of Rhaegar's father, Aerys II, a prince of Dornish blood might very well have one day ruled the realm..." However, two kings with Dornish blood already ruled Westeros, Aerys I Targaryen and Maekar I Targaryen, the sons of King Daeron II Targaryen and his wife, Queen Myriah Martell.

Other Lands[ ]

It is stated we know "nothing at all about whatever lands may lie beyond the Last Light and across the Sunset Sea", where "Last Light" is probably a misspelling of Lonely Light.

The Free Cities[ ]

In the chapter on Norvos, Lomas Longstrider is misspelled as Loras. This should already be corrected in later editions.

The chapter describing Pentos states that the Pentoshi would give gifts and gold to all Dothraki khals "who brought their khalasars east of the Rhoyne." However, Pentos is located to the west of the Rhoyne.

In the chapter about Myr, Lys, and Tyrosh, it is said Larra Rogare was nineteen when her father Lysandro made her marry Viserys II Targaryen: since Viserys was born in 122 AC and Larra was seven years his senior, that would place their marriage in 134 AC. However, Fire & Blood, which says Lysandro Rogare quickly changed plans about Viserys after learning the identity of the prince, implies a faster timeline. Elio García has clarified that The World of Ice & Fire is incorrect and a more accurate date for Viserys and Larra's wedding would be 132 or 133 AC.

In the chapter on Braavos, the passage describing the Uncloaking of Uthero says that 111 years after the founding of Braavos, the "existence and location" of the city was made known to the world. However, this contradicts A Feast for Crows, which states that the city's "existence had been a secret for a century", but "its location had been hidden thrice that long"; as well as an earlier chapter of TWOIAF, which states that "for centuries" Braavos had remained hidden from the world.

In the chapter on Braavos, a reference is made to "the imposing Palace of Justice". However, in accordance with A Feast for Crows and The Lands of Ice and Fire, this is supposed to be "the imposing Palace of Truth".

In the chapter on Braavos, Bellegere Otherys is introduced as "who reigned briefly as one of the nine paramours of King Aegon IV Targaryen", which is inaccurate, since Aegon was still prince when he was with her.

Beyond the Free Cities[ ]

Gogossos is misspelled as Gorgossos in some earlier prints of  The World of Ice & Fire.

The city Sathar is stated to be located at the juncture of two branches of the Sarne. On the maps of The Lands of Ice and Fire Sathar is not located on the Sarne, but on a separate river that flows north into the Bay of Tusks. The maps from The Lands of Ice and Fire show two rivers replacing the dried up sea mentioned in The World of Ice & Fire. The Lands of Ice and Fire is in error.

The chapter on Ib says the Dothraki invaded "two hundred years ago", which should be "four hundred years ago", since it later mentioned Khal Dhako and Khal Temmo, both living during Century of Blood.

The chapter on Asshai says "the Sea Snake never saw Asshai"', which is incorrect as Fire & Blood states he was the first Westerosi to ever lie eyes on Asshai, on his second trip on the Sea Snake.

Gallard is described as a Maester whereas he is stated to be one of the Archmaester of the Citadel in the appendix of A Feast for Crows.

Appendix[ ]

Targaryen Lineage:

The Targaryen family tree has Aerea Targaryen, Cassana Estermont, Rhaenys Targaryen (Rhaegar's daughter), and Gael Targaryen listed as males, though they are all actually female.

Rhaella Targaryen's name is misspelled as Rhalla Targaryen in earlier prints, which was corrected in later prints.

The birth order of Jaehaerys and Alysanne's children is rearranged in Fire & Blood. And Aeryn Targaryen is changed to Daenerys Targaryen.

Daenaera Velaryon's name is misspelled as Valeryon.

Laenor Velaryon is listed on the family tree to have been older than his sister Laena, but he was actually the younger sibling, as described in The Rogue Prince, and on GRRM's Not a Blog.

Larra Rogare is misspelled as "Lara Rogare".

Spellings of Robar Baratheon and Mariah Martell's names are changed to Rogar and Myriah in later prints.

Stark Lineage:

Jocelyn Stark is mentioned to have married "Benedict Rodgers". However, this is supposed to be Benedict Royce. According to Elio Garcia, co-author of The World of Ice & Fire, Martin mistakenly called Royce "Benedict Vance" in earlier notes. However, Harrold Rogers, the husband of Branda Stark, is correct.

Munkun's True Telling says Cregan Stark had a younger brother who died in 119 AC. However, in the family tree Cregan has no siblings.

For Rickon Stark's daughters Serena Stark and Sansa Stark, Serena is marked as the elder one in the book. However, Sansa is the elder one in George R. R. Martin's notes.

For Edric Stark's twin sons Cregard Stark and Torrhen Stark, Cregard is listed as the first one in the book, which should be indicate that Cregard is the elder brother. However, Torrhen is listed as the first one in George R. R. Martin's notes, which should be indicate that Torrhen is the elder brother.

Bolded names in the family tree indicate that said person has ruled as Lord of Winterfell. However, the names of Rodwell Stark and Donnor Stark are not bolded, even though they had been Lords of Winterfell. This should have been corrected by the third print.

Myriah Stark is wrongly spelled as Mariah in earlier prints.

Argelle Stark is wrongly spelled as "Aregelle".

Lannister Lineage:

Jaime Lannister's name is misspelled as "Jamie".

Joffrey Baratheon's name is misspelled as "Joffery".

Myrielle Lannister's name is misspelled as "Myielle".

Willem and Martyn Lannister are not listed to be twins, even though they have been stated to be twins on multiple occasions elsewhere.

Bolded names in the family tree indicate that said person has ruled as Lord of Casterly Rock. Neither Cersei nor Cerelle Lannister are marked as "ruling lord or kings". As both have been specifically stated to have been "Lady of Casterly Rock", this is an omission. Kevan Lannister's name is bolded, while he was never a ruling Lord. As Cersei has remained "Lady of Casterly Rock" despite the charges of high treason, and has two heirs who would inherit from her before Kevan would, even if she was stripped from her rights to Casterly Rock, Kevan's name being bolded likely is a mistake.

Melesa Crakehall, the wife of Lyonel Frey, is missing from the family tree.

Reign of the Kings:

Baelor I Targaryen and Daeron I Targaryen are switched in the overview of the Targaryen Kings. The reign of Baelor I Targaryen is erroneously listed to have been from 157 AC to 161 AC, and the reign of Daeron I Targaryen from 161 AC until 171 AC.

In the first print, the overview of reigns states that Joffrey Baratheon's reign ended in 299 AC. However, he died on the first day of 300 AC
