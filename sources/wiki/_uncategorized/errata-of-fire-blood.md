Background[ ]

In his A Song of Ice and Fire series, George R. R. Martin uses the "unreliable narrator". As such, not all inconsistencies in the books that are mistakes. However, Martin has expressed that accidental mistakes can distract from the planned inconsistencies, making them less effective. As such, the pages indexed here represent a collection of gathered possible and confirmed mistakes and, if possible, their confirmed corrections within Martin's series.

What you will not find on this page are discussions of information from the So Spake Martins which contradicts with more recently released printed material, contradictions found in sample chapters from unreleased books (e.g. The Winds of Winter), or speculations about possible solutions.

Examples of what will not be found on the errata pages include:

According to Osha, "the blue star in the rider's eye" of the constellation the Ice Dragon points north. However, when Bran Stark later recalls Osha's statement, he does so incorrectly: "The blue star in the dragon's eye pointed the way north, as Osha told him once."

Differences in views on certain events. While the "commonly told story about the Battle of the Bells" apparently states that Robert I Baratheon and Jon Connington did not come face to face during the battle (as stated by Maester Yandel ["Rightly famed is Robert's grand victory at Stoney Sept, also called the Battle of the Bells, where he slew the famous Ser Myles Mooton—once Prince Rhaegar's squire—and five men besides, and might well have killed the new Hand, Lord Connington, had the battle brought them together."] and Harwin of Winterfell ["Robert came out of hiding to join the fight when the bells began to ring. He slew six men that day, they say. One was Myles Mooton, a famous knight who'd been Prince Rhaegar's squire. He would have slain the Hand too, but the battle never brought them together."]), Connington himself recalls it rather differently ("Bells and battle followed, and Robert emerged from his brothel with a blade in hand, and almost slew Jon on the steps of the old sept that gave the town its name.").

Fire & Blood[ ]

Reign of the Dragon - The Wars of King Aegon I[ ]

It is indicated that "every castle" in Dorne was burned thrice in the Dragon's Wroth, including Sunspear, and that Visenya had burned it earlier, but the ancient keep of House Martell, the Sandship, did not show any signs of burning. George R. R. Martin agreed this was an issue, and expanded on it by providing the maesters several speculative explanations for why the Targaryens left Sunspear alone, as explained in The World of Ice & Fire. However, when Fire & Blood was published, old drafts were used for this part, so Sunspear was also burned. The error is corrected in later edition.

Three Heads Had the Dragon - Governance Under King Aegon I[ ]

Adjudicated is misspelled as ajudicated once in the UK version (UK hardcover, page 44. The error is not present in the US version).

The first Grand Maester, Archmaester Ollidar, was the "keeper of histories, whose ring and rod and mask were bronze." However, copper is the metal for the regalia of the historian archmaesters, and bronze for stargazing, per the appendix of A Feast for Crows.

The Sons of the Dragon[ ]

It is said King Aegon I Targaryen celebrated his sixtieth nameday in 33 AC. However, since he was born in the year 27 BC and had conquered the Seven Kingdoms at the age of twenty-seven, in 1 AC, after a war of two years (2 BC-1 AC), Aegon was actually fifty-nine years old in 33 AC.

The crown of King Aegon I Targaryen has been described in So Spake Martin as "a simple circle of Valyrian steel set with big square-cut rubies" and in the "Aegon's Conquest" chapter of Fire & Blood as a "Valyrian steel circlet, studded with rubies. However, "The Sons of the Dragon" chapter incorrectly describes the crown as being "iron-and-ruby."

While the novella The Sons of the Dragon says "Though half a dozen hatchlings had been born amidst the fires of Dragonstone in the later years of Aegon’s reign.", Fire & Blood states "Though a dozen hatchlings had been born amidst the fires of Dragonstone in the later years of Aegon's reign".

The World of Ice & Fire states that the Kingsguard Ser Raymont Baratheon saved Aenys I Targaryen from a murder attempt by two Poor Fellows. However, the name of the Kingsguard who saved Aenys is not given in Fire & Blood. It is suggested not to name Raymont explicitly in future editions of The World of Ice & Fire.

House Myatt is removed from the pious houses that Maegor burned the seats of in the westerlands.

It is said that no Dornish lords supported the Vulture King during the Second Dornish War, but Lord Walter Wyl is known to have supported him. Later edition corrects "no" to "few".

It is claimed that Jaehaerys Targaryen, instead of Aerea Targaryen, was "the rightful heir by all the laws of the Seven Kingdoms". However, a daughter comes before an uncle according to customs. In the novella version, the expression is that Jaehaerys was "felt by many to be the rightful heir".

Prince into King - The Ascension of Jaehaerys I[ ]

It is said the dungeons of King's Landing were "emptied" in 48 AC, following the end of Maegor I's reign. However, when Jaehaerys Targaryen emptied the dungeons again in 50 AC, there were still men imprisoned by Maegor I. Later edition corrects it to "largely emptied".

The Year of The Three Brides - 49 AC[ ]

Queen Alyssa Velaryon and her children are said to have fled from King's Landing when discussing Lord Rogar Baratheon giving his support to Jaehaerys Targaryen. This should be "Dragonstone", which is corrected in later editions.

Alyssa Velaryon is said to have been forced to watch her daughter Rhaena Targaryen's wedding to King Maegor I Targaryen in 47 AC, which could not literally have happened as Alyssa had fled beyond Maegor's reach by that point.

It is remarked before the Golden Wedding (which took place on seventh day of the seventh moon in 49 AC) that Jaehaerys' sixteenth nameday was "less than a year away". However, Jaehaerys' sixteenth nameday was on the twentieth day of the ninth moon in 50 AC, more than a year later. It is corrected in later edition.

A Time of Testing - The Realm Remade[ ]

When describing the seven speakers that Jaehaerys sends to preach Exceptionalism, the septons are described with a contradiction for Septon Alfyn, as he is called a "young septon", but in the same sentence also "fierce old Septon Alfyn": "Three were young septons; cunning Septon Baldrick, learned Septon Rollo, and fierce old Septon Alfyn". It is corrected in later edition.

It is stated "the year (51 AC) continued without further crisis or test as Jaehaerys and Alysanne settled in to rule". However, Alysanne was almost killed in Maidenpool at the end of 51 AC in next chapter. In fact, this chapter was supposed to happen in 50 AC in the draft. It is corrected to "the better part of the year" in later edition.

Jaehaerys and Alysanne - Their Triumphs and Tragedies[ ]

"Cove" is misspelled as "cover" once in the text: "this was a vessel meant for deep waters, not bays and covers and inland shallows."

The Long Reign - Jaehaerys and Alysanne: Policy, Progeny, and Pain[ ]

When discussing Corlys Velaryon and the wealth of House Velaryon, House Velaryon is said to have become the wealthiest house of the Seven Kingdoms for a while following the first voyage. Later on, it is stated that House Velaryon became the wealthiest house following Corlys's ninth voyage. Later edition states "House Velaryon doubled its wealth from the profits of that single journey" instead.

Prince Gaemon Targaryen was born in 73 AC and died "died a few days into the new year, not quite three moons old". However, the context suggested he died at the same year. Later edition states "Prince Gaemon died just short of the new year".

The name of Prince Valerion Targaryen is once misspelled as "Velarian" (US hardcover, page 305), which is corrected in later editions.

Prince Vaegon Targaryen is called the third son of Jaehaerys I and Alysanne Targaryen, while he actually is the fourth son of the king and queen, having two older living brothers (Aemon and Baelon) and one deceased older brother, Aegon. It is corrected in later edition.

Heirs of the Dragon - A Question of Succession[ ]

Princess Rhaenys Targaryen is said to have learned that she was pregnant shortly before the ninth day of the third month of 92 AC. That would suggest that the child would be born, following a nine month pregnancy, in 92 AC as well. However, the child, Laena Velaryon is specified to have been born in 93 AC. Later edition moves her birth year to 92 AC.

Daemon Targaryen is frequently referred to as "the rogue prince" in the namesake novella. The title is never mentioned in Fire & Blood.

In The Rogue Prince it is said Mushroom claimed that Daemon deflowered Alicent Hightower, whereas in Fire & Blood that rumor is replaced by Mushroom suggesting an intimate relationship between Alicent and Jaehaerys I Targaryen.

In The World of Ice & Fire and The Rogue Prince it is said that Laenor Velaryon has "dragon blood on both sides of his lineage". However, this is not mentioned in Fire & Blood.

Lord Boremund Baratheon is incorrectly referred to as the great-uncle of Princess Rhaenys Targaryen and the great-great-uncle of her son, Laenor Velaryon (US hardcover, page 350), while he is, in fact, the uncle of Rhaenys, and the great-uncle of Laenor. It is fixed in later edition.

Archmaester Vaegon is said to be forty years old in 101 AC, which is incorrect. He was born in 63 AC, which made him thirty-eight (or thirty-seven, depending when exactly the man was born in the year 63 AC) at the Great Council of 101 AC. It is fixed in later edition.

The Dying of the Dragons[ ]

The Valyrian steel crown of King Aegon I Targaryen is again incorrectly described as being "iron-and-ruby." It is fixed in later edition.

Munkun's name is misspelled as Munken once.

Jeyne Arryn at the age of thirty-five was described as twenty years older than Jacaerys Velaryon, whose age was given as fourteen not long ago (incorrectly given as fifteen in The Princess and the Queen). In fact, Jacaerys was born at the end of 114 AC, making him fourteen at the beginning of the Dance of the Dragons at the first half of 129 AC. It is fixed in later edition.

Munkun's True Telling says Cregan Stark had a younger brother who died in 119 AC. However, the Stark family tree shows Cregan had no siblings.

Borros Baratheon referred to Princess Rhaenys Targaryen as "Princess Rhaenys is kin to me and mine, some great-aunt I never knew was married to her father", while Rhaenys's mother Jocelyn Baratheon is, in fact, the aunt of Borros.

The name of the Bastard of Lannisport, Ser Tyler Hill, has been changed to Ser Emory Hill.

Provenance is misspelled as provenence once in the UK version (UK hardcover, page 481. The error is not present in the US version).

Laena Velaryon is twice called Daemon Targaryen's " first wife". But she's his second wife. The former one is fixed in later edition.

When Ormund Hightower died, it is stated that "his sons remained back at the Hightower". However, later it revealed only Lyonel Hightower was at Hightower, the other two sons were at the Arbor and Highgarden.

Hobert Hightower is once referred to as "Hobart", and Bold Jon Roxton is once referred to as "Bold John Roxton".

Ser Balon Byrch was introduced as the captain at the Old Gate, but he was called "captain at the Iron Gate" the next day.

"Willum Royce" has been changed into Willam Royce.

Vermithor, born in 34 AC, is described, before and after Vhagar's death, as the oldest and largest dragon at the time of the Dance of the Dragons right after Vhagar (not counting the controversial case of the Cannibal). However, since Dreamfyre, a dragon that did not take part in the conflict, was born before Vermithor, and died before Vermithor but after Vhagar, it should be said Vermithor was the second oldest dragon among those fighting in the war and that became the oldest living dragon after both Vhagar and Dreamfyre's death.

The son of Lord Borros Baratheon and Lady Elenda Caron is named "Olyver Baratheon" on numerous occasions, and said to have been named for Lady Elenda's father. However, Lady Elenda's father is identified as "Royce Caron". Furthermore, elsewhere Olyver's name is misspelled as "Olyvar". Later edition changes his name to Royce.

Trystane Truefyre is once referred to as "Trystan".

The novella "The Princess and the Queen" says Aegon II declared "in all chronicles and court records, his half sister be referred to only as princess". However, no such order presents in Fire & Blood.

The House of Kisses is once erroneously stated to be located on the Street of Silk on Hill of Rhaenys. Instead, it is located on the Street of Sisters atop of Visenya's Hill.

Aftermath - The Hour of the Wolf[ ]

When the riverlords entered King's Landing two days after King Aegon II Targaryen's death, they were welcomed by "Ser Gyles Belgrave and four other Kingsguard". However, later during Cregan Stark's trials, it is revealed there were only four Kingsguard in King's Landing: "Three of Belgrave's Sworn Brothers had been present at King Aegon's death and were similarly condemned, though their complicity in the plot could not be proved (the three Kingsguard who were not in the city were judged innocent)." Later edition changes it to "Ser Gyles Belgrave and three other Kingsguard".

It is said that, after the death of King Aegon II, Lord Leowyn Corbray went ashore at Duskendale and Ser Corwyn Corbray at Maidenpool, each one with half the army sent by Lady Jeyne Arryn, supporting the claim of King Aegon III; however, the text later reports that Leowyn departed Maidenpool, while Corwyn departed Duskendale. It should be noted that, instead, The Rise of the Dragon states only that Leowyn landed at Duskendale and Corwyn at Maidenpool.

Under the Regents[ ]

The son of Lord Borros Baratheon and Lady Elenda Caron is named "Olyver Baratheon" on numerous occasions, and said to have been named for Lady Elenda's father. However, Lady Elenda's father is identified as "Royce Caron". Furthermore, elsewhere Olyver's name is misspelled as "Olyvar". Later edition changes his name to Royce.

Royce Caron is referred to as "Marshal of the Marches", which is changed to "Lord of the Marches" in later edition, so as to be consistent with other materials.

It is claimed the Three Widows (Johanna Lannister, Elenda Baratheon, and Samantha Hightower) were the first to leave after King Aegon III's wedding. However, only Johanna could leave at that time, since Elenda didn't come to the wedding, and Samantha's leave was described later. It is fixed in later edition.

When the council discussed whether Rhaena or Baela should be Aegon's heir, Leowyn Corbray first said "Lady Rhaena would make a splendid queen," then said "Baela flew a dragon, Rhaena only has the hatchling," contradicting himself. It seems the latter claim could be from Manfryd Mooton, the only regent present whose voice was not recorded. Later edition confirms it is from Manfryd Mooton.

It is stated Ser Willis Fell and two other kingsguards died in the Winter Fever, also the naming of Regis Groves' successor was interrupted by the Winter Fever, leaving four empty positions in the kingguard. However, both King Aegon III and Unwin Peake only filled two empty positions later. Later edition keeps Willis's companions alive.

In 133 AC, it is claimed Unwin Peake "would rule the Seven Kingdoms in all but name for the best part of three years, serving as Lord Regent, Protector of the Realm, and Hand of the King." However, he only ruled as Lord Regent, Protector of the Realm, and Hand of the King for nearly two years, till 134 AC. It is fixed in later edition.

At one place "gallery" is misspelled as "galley" ("She sparkled," Mushroom says, "and when she smiled, the singers in the galley rejoiced, for they knew that here at last was a maid worthy of a song").

Corwyn Corbray is incorrectly referred to as "Lord Corbray" once (The court rejoiced when Lady Rhaena announced that she was with child by Lord Corbray).  It is changed to "Lord Corwyn Corbray" in later edition.

Dalton Greyjoy claimed four of Lord Farman’s daughters as salt wives, however, later it says he had only taken two of the late Lord Farman’s daughters. It is changed to "two of the late Lord Farman's surviving daughters" in later edition.

The Lysene Spring and the End of Regency[ ]

It is stated Manfryd Mooton is "the last of King Aegon’s original regents". However, Grand Maester Munkun is the last of King Aegon's original regents. A "nearly" is added in later edition.

Elenda Caron's given name is two times misspelled 'Elendra'.

"Forbearance" is misspelled as "forebearance" ("Many and more have wondered at the seeming forebearance of the new Hand and his allies during this stalemate."), and "voluble" misspelled as "voluable" ("Ser Gareth Long was the most voluable").

Robert Rowan is referred to as "Lord" ("High in the Mountains, the unthinkable happened one night as Lord Robert and his men huddled about in their campfires"). However, as Robert's father, Lord Rowan, is still alive, Robert should not have been given the title "Lord".  It is fixed in later edition.

It is stated George Graceford named Ser Victor Risley among the conspirators. However, later it revealed Ser Gareth Long was the "first and foremost of those who had named Risley part of the plot". Later edition changes it to "foremost of those who had named Risley part of the plot".

Lineages and Family Tree[ ]

The appendix incorrectly states that King Daeron II Targaryen brought Dorne into the realm by marrying Myriah Martell, while it is known that Dorne only joined the Seven Kingdoms when Prince Maron Martell married Princess Daenerys Targaryen in 187 AC. It is fixed in later edition.

Princess Myriah Martell's name, which was "Myriah" in the main series, but changed into "Mariah" in The World of Ice & Fire, has been changed back to "Myriah Martell".
