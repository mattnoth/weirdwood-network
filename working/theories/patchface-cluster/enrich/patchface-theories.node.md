## Claim

The theory holds that Patchface did not merely survive the shipwreck that killed
Steffon and Cassana Baratheon — he drowned in it, like the hundred sailors and
soldiers who never came back up, and was resurrected days later by the Drowned God to
serve as an undead prophet. On this reading, the two "lost days" the text never
explains are the time he spent dead, and the nonsense songs he sings afterward are not
a broken mind's babble but literal, garbled prophecy: cryptic previews of the Red
Wedding, the Purple Wedding, and the Battle of the Blackwater, and — more
speculatively — a hint that he is aligned with the side of the dead and may have a
role to play at Hardhome. Two further, more speculative extensions (held out of this
node's evidence, see Ungrounded material) read Patchface as connected to
Lovecraft-style "Deep Ones" fish-hybrids, and as a possible vector for spreading
Shireen's dormant greyscale into a plague.

## Evidence For

**The resurrection itself is the theory's premise, already established as book fact**
(not itself an edge — see the `patchface` character node's Origins section for the
full account): Patchface washed up three days after the wreck, seemingly dead, then
"coughed water and sat up"; no one ever explained what happened to him during the two
days he was lost at sea; he came back "broken in body and mind," having lost "half his
wits and all his memory." What the theory adds beyond this established fact is the
specific claim that this resurrection was the Drowned God's doing, made for a
prophetic purpose — and *that* claim rests on the doctrinal parallel below, not on the
shipwreck story alone.

**The ironborn drowning-and-revival doctrine.** The Drowned God's own faith
deliberately drowns and revives converts as a mark of rebirth in the god's service:

> "Have courage," he said. "We came from the sea, and to the sea we must return. Open
> your mouth and drink deep of god's blessing. Fill your lungs with water, that you
> may die and be reborn."
— sources/chapters/affc/affc-the-prophet-01.md:13 (edge P7, node aeron-greyjoy)

Patchface's shipwreck-drowning-then-revival was not a deliberate ritual — he did not
choose it, and Aeron did not perform it on him — so the parallel is analogical, not
literal identity with the ironborn rite. The theory leans on the resemblance anyway:
gods in the series who mark undead champions (Beric Dondarrion for R'hllor, Bloodraven
and Coldhands for the old gods) suggest a Drowned-God parallel is thematically
consistent with how the books handle resurrection-as-service elsewhere, even though
that broader pattern argument was not independently grounded to citable text in this
pass (see Ungrounded material).

**Patchface's songs echo ironborn afterlife doctrine he has no ordinary way of
knowing.** At the wedding of Sigorn and Alys Karstark:

> "Under the sea the mermen feast on starfish soup, and all the serving men are
> crabs," Patchface proclaimed as they went. "I know, I know, oh, oh, oh."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-jon-10.md:117 (edge P4, node patchface)

This matches the ironborn's own description of the Drowned God's watery halls, where
the dead feast for eternity — lore a Volantis-born slave-turned-fool would have no
ordinary route to. Tier-4: circumstantial (a thematic echo, not a direct prophecy of a
specific event).

**Melisandre's vision independently flags Patchface as significant and dangerous** —
the strongest single piece of "a character in-text treats him as real" evidence, from
the same chapter as the mermen-feast song:

> Melisandre's face darkened. "That creature is dangerous. Many a time I have
> glimpsed him in my flames. Sometimes there are skulls about him, and his lips are
> red with blood."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-jon-10.md:119 (edge P5, node melisandre)

Melisandre is a demonstrated seer whose visions have been shown elsewhere to be
accurate (if often misread); her unprompted, specific alarm about a supposedly
harmless "lackwit" fool is read by the theory as validating that something real and
dark is happening with Patchface.

**Moqorro's characterization of the Drowned God itself.** A second red priest
independently frames the god Patchface would serve as aligned with the side of death:

> "Your Drowned God is a demon," the black priest Moqorro said afterward. "He is no
> more than a thrall of the Other, the dark god whose name must not be spoken."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-victarion-01.md:73 (edge P6, node moqorro — the edge
quotes the second sentence only, since the first is split from it by the narration tag)

Tier-4: this is Moqorro's assessment of the Drowned God broadly, one inferential step
removed from a direct statement about Patchface himself.

**Two song beats read as specific, dated predictions.** In Book 3, months before the
Red Wedding, Patchface sings to Davos on Dragonstone:

> "Fool's blood, king's blood, blood on the maiden's thigh, but chains for the guests
> and chains for the bridegroom, aye aye aye."
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-davos-02.md:201 (edge P1, node patchface)

The theory reads this as naming, in order: a fool's death (Aegon Frey, called "Jinglebell," has the wits of a small child and dies in the massacre), a king's death (Robb Stark), the deflowering of the bride (Roslin Frey, on her wedding night, in the same hall), and captives put in irons (Edmure Tully and the surviving Stark bannermen) — a match the video and this node's `strength_as_presented` source data both flag as the strongest of the set. Separately, in Book 2, well before the Purple Wedding, Patchface sings:

> Patchface rang his bells. "It is always summer under the sea," he intoned. "The
> merwives wear nennymoans in their hair and weave gowns of silver seaweed. I know, I
> know, oh, oh, oh."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-prologue.md:61 (edge P2, node patchface)

The theory reads "nennymoans in their hair" and "silver seaweed" gowns as pointing to
Sansa Stark's silver hair net (concealing the poison used to kill Joffrey) and her
silver-and-white wedding gown at the Purple Wedding. Tier-4: looser wordplay than the
Red Wedding beat, resting on a color/hair-ornament association rather than a close
narrative match.

**A third beat, from Book 5, is read as identifying with the side of the dead rather
than predicting a specific already-occurred event.** When Jon Snow announces the
ranging to Hardhome and asks for a volunteer to lead it:

> Patchface jumped up. "I will lead it!" His bells rang merrily. "We will march into
> the sea and out again. Under the waves we will ride seahorses, and mermaids will
> blow seashells to announce our coming, oh, oh, oh."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-jon-13.md:39 (edge P3, node patchface)

The theory reads "march into the sea and out again" as dying and rising again — and
notes Patchface's repeated use of "we" and "our," read as him speaking for the side of
the dead rather than the living. This is the theory's most direct textual link between
Patchface and the still-unresolved Hardhome catastrophe (see `hardhome-catastrophe`),
though whether he ever actually leads or joins that mission is unconfirmed in the
published books.

## Evidence Against

The theory is structurally difficult to falsify within the published text: Patchface
speaks only in songs the narrative never has any character definitively decode, so
every mapping from song lyric to real-world event is the theory's own retrospective
pattern-matching, applied after the predicted event is already known to the reader.
None of the "predicted" events (Red Wedding, Purple Wedding) has a character in-text
confirm that Patchface foresaw them; the parallels are compelling but circumstantial.

Whether Patchface will play any active role at Hardhome specifically is unconfirmed:
the Hardhome catastrophe itself is known to Jon Snow (and the reader) only through
Cotter Pyke's letter — an unwitnessed, reported disaster, not something the published
books have depicted on the page (see `hardhome-catastrophe`). Patchface's own
volunteering line (edge P3) shows intent and self-identification with "the sea," not a
confirmed future role.

## Ungrounded material (outside the corpus)

- **book-speculative** — Patchface's "Under the sea, smoke rises in bubbles, and
  flames burn green and blue and black" is read by the source video as a coded
  warning of the Battle of the Blackwater's green wildfire. Byte-verification located
  this exact line at `sources/chapters/acok/acok-davos-01.md:43` — but that scene is
  the burning of the Seven statues and Stannis drawing "Lightbringer" from the pyre
  (jade-green flames included), not the Blackwater battle at all; the character node
  already attributes this same recurring line to that scene. The Blackwater reading
  has no textual anchor placing it at that battle and was deliberately **not** made an
  edge here.
- **community / Lovecraft parallel (sub-claim 2, held out in full)** — The "Deep Ones"
  fish-hybrid reading rests on an explicitly out-of-book GRRM/Lovecraft influence claim
  (ruined cities named "K'dath," "Carcosa," "Leng"), plus Westerosi folklore about
  half-fish "squishers" (`sources/chapters/affc/affc-brienne-04.md:87`) and
  fisherfolk gossip that a mermaid taught Patchface "to breathe water in return for
  his seed" (`sources/chapters/acok/acok-prologue.md:91` — already catalogued
  verbatim on the `patchface` character node's Origins section, so not re-edged here
  as new theory evidence). None of these connect to Patchface with anything beyond
  thematic/word-association resonance; the Lovecraft-name claim itself is
  not-groundable against the 5-book corpus.
- **community / self-hedged (sub-claim 3, held out in full)** — The greyscale-plague
  reading chains together Shireen's established greyscale backstory
  (`sources/chapters/asos/asos-davos-05.md:221`), Val's warning that she is "not
  clean" (`sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-jon-11.md:223`), the Shrouded Lord/Garin
  water-magic curse lore (`sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-tyrion-05.md:47`), and
  Shireen's dreams of being eaten by stone dragons
  (`sources/chapters/acok/acok-prologue.md:45`) into a prediction that Patchface will
  help spread a plague. Each individual beat is a real, grounded quote, but the chain
  connecting them is the video's own synthesis; the source itself calls this thread
  "pretty vague." No edges were made for this sub-claim.
- **book-interpretive** — "Only death can pay for life" (Daenerys to Mirri Maz Duur,
  `sources/chapters/agot/agot-daenerys-10.md:95`) is used by the video as an
  analogical cross-reference — the hundred Baratheon men who drowned alongside
  Patchface read as the "sacrifice" that paid for his resurrection. Real quote, but
  from an unconnected scene; a pattern argument, not direct evidence about Patchface.
- **book-interpretive** — Melisandre's broader R'hllor-versus-the-Great-Other cosmology
  speech to Davos (`sources/chapters/asos/asos-davos-03.md:69`) sets up the general
  two-sided cosmic war the theory places Patchface within, but the passage itself is
  not about Patchface and was not made an edge.
- **community** — Aeron "Damphair" Greyjoy performing the ironborn drowning ritual on
  a convert (`sources/chapters/affc/affc-the-prophet-01.md:13,29`, edge P7) is used by
  the source video to argue Patchface is the "real" prophet by contrast with Aeron,
  whom the video characterizes as never having produced an actual prophecy. That
  broader characterization of Aeron's track record spans many chapters and was not
  independently grounded to a single quote in this pass; only the ritual-doctrine
  passage itself was edged (see edge P7 above).
- **GRRM / TWOW** — The video closes on the unreleased *Winds of Winter*
  promotional line "Old powers waken. Shadows stir. An age of wonder and terror will
  soon be upon us." Outside the published 5-book corpus; not groundable.

## Status Notes

**Show:** Game of Thrones never adapted Patchface — the character does not appear in
the show at all. There is no show outcome to report; status stays `open`, and there is
no `show-confirmed` possibility for this theory (contrast `r-plus-l-equals-j`, which
the show did resolve).

**ASX verdict:** the source video presents the core resurrection/prophet claim as
likely ("it all adds up — it's likely that Patchface was resurrected to serve as a
prophet of the Drowned God"), while explicitly flagging the three further sub-theories
(Hardhome, Deep Ones, greyscale plague) as speculative — "it's all pretty vague, but
it could fit." This node's edge set reflects that split: the core claim carries two
tier-3 edges (the Red Wedding song, Melisandre's vision) plus five tier-4
circumstantial/analogical edges; the two most speculative sub-claims (Deep Ones,
greyscale plague) carry no edges at all.
