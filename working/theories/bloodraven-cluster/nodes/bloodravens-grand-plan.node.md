## Claim

The theory holds that Brynden Rivers — Lord Bloodraven, and since roughly 252 AC the
last greenseer living beyond the Wall as "the three-eyed crow" — is not merely
Bran Stark's magical mentor but the architect of a long, hidden design. Three
claim-strands, treated by the source video as facets of one picture, are absorbed
into this single node: (1) that Brynden actively works, through the weirwood network
and (the theory speculates) remote control of Lord Commander Mormont's raven, to
shape Jon Snow's rise and Bran's greenseer training toward some larger purpose tied
to the Targaryen family's generations-long fixation on the Azor Ahai prophecy; (2)
that this same pattern of concealment, manipulation, and absolute political power —
demonstrated across his mortal life as Hand of the King and continued in his
treatment of Bran — is at least as consistent with Brynden being a villain (or an
agent of something worse than the Others) as with him being a hidden hero; and (3) a
weaker, more speculative extension that Brynden's known habit of "auditioning" gifted
dreamers may once have reached as far as a young Euron Greyjoy. The theory does not
resolve which reading is correct — the video's own conclusion is that the ambiguity
is likely deliberate.

## Evidence For

**Established premise (not itself an edge — see brynden-rivers.node.md for the
tier-1/2 record).** Brynden's biography already carries the raw material both
readings work from: his mixed Valyrian/First-Men parentage (Aegon IV Targaryen ×
Melissa Blackwood), his D&E-era rule as Hand of the King and master of whisperers
("with spells and spies"), the kinslaying of Daemon Blackfyre and two of his sons at
the Redgrass Field, the "thousand eyes, and one" riddle, and — in ADWD — his
transformation into "half-corpse and half-tree," fused into a weirwood throne, tutor
to Bran in greensight and skinchanging. None of this is re-argued here as new
evidence; the theory's move is entirely in how it is *read*.

**Leaf frames Brynden's purpose as larger than himself.** When Bran first reaches the
cave, Meera's companion Leaf explains Brynden's condition and stake in the world:

> He has lived beyond his mortal span, and yet he lingers. For us, for you, for the
> realms of men.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:25 (edge G1, node brynden-rivers)

The theory reads "for the realms of men" as confirmation that Brynden's presence
beyond the Wall serves a stated purpose beyond simply outliving his body — the seed
of the "grand plan" reading.

**Mormont's raven's fixation on Jon's name.** Twice more after Ned's Tower of Joy-era
generation, the raven that once belonged to Lord Commander Mormont behaves oddly
around Jon specifically. During a tense food distribution at the gate with wildling
refugees (not, as the source video states, at a Lord Commander election — corrected
here against the substrate):

> Mormont’s raven walked from Jon’s left shoulder to his right, bobbing its head and
> muttering, “Snow, snow, snow.”
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-jon-05.md:105 (prose only — S216 fresh-verify REJECTED the proposed edge: a raven muttering "Snow" is what a bird of previous riders does; the text offers no Brynden anchor for the remote-influence reading)

Later, on the morning Jon opens the Wall's gate to the wildlings:

> “Corn,” the bird said, and, “King,” and, “Snow, Jon Snow, Jon Snow.” That was
> queer. The bird had never said his full name before, as best Jon could recall.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-jon-12.md:25 (edge G3, node jon-snow)

The narration's own "That was queer" is the strongest in-text hook: the text flags
the raven's behavior as anomalous rather than merely comic mimicry, which the theory
reads as evidence of intentional (Brynden-directed) influence rather than a talking
bird's random vocabulary.

**A prophetic-dreaming bloodline that runs wider than the direct royal line.**
Brynden's own explanation to Dunk and Egg of how Daemon Blackfyre — a rival claimant,
not a crowned Targaryen — could still have a true prophetic dream:

> "There have always been Targaryens who dreamed of things to come, since long before
> the Conquest," Bloodraven said, "so we should not be surprised if from time to time
> a Blackfyre displays the gift as well. Daemon dreamed that a dragon would be born
> at Whitewalls, and it was. The fool just got the color wrong."
— sources/chapters/tmk/tmk-dunk-01.md:2039 (edge G4, node brynden-rivers)

The opening clause is already quoted (wiki-sourced, no book cite) on
`brynden-rivers.node.md`; this edge book-cites that existing fact and extends it with
the Whitewalls/Daemon material, which is the theory's actual anchor for treating
prophetic dreaming as a trait Brynden tracks across the wider Targaryen/Blackfyre
bloodline, not just the throne's direct line — feeding the "breeding program" strand.

**The Azor Ahai prophecy already drove one royal marriage.** Ser Barristan explains
to Daenerys why her parents, Aerys and Rhaella, were wed without love:

> “Your grandsire commanded it. A woods witch had told him that the prince was
> promised would be born of their line.”
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-daenerys-04.md:193 (prose only — S216 adjudication DROPPED the proposed edge: prophecy-driven dynastic marriage is premise with no Bloodraven anchor in the passage; the beat family lives on the azor-ahai-theories cluster)

This book-cites a fact already stated (wiki-only) on `aerys-ii-targaryen.node.md`:
Jaehaerys II arranged Aerys and Rhaella's marriage on a woods witch's prophecy of
Azor Ahai's bloodline. The theory treats this as one confirmed instance of prophecy
directly shaping a Targaryen marriage, extending Brynden's own prophecy-obsessed
household (his and Shiera Seastar's interest in "ancient scrolls" and "dusty
prophecies" is asserted by the source video but was **not** independently grounded to
a citable passage this pass — see Ungrounded material) into a family-wide pattern the
theory reads him as exploiting or extending.

**Brynden's absolute, unchecked political power.** Septon Sefton's drunken rant to
Dunk is the clearest textual statement of just how total Brynden's control of the
realm was as Hand of the King:

> "Make no mistake, 'tis Lord Rivers who rules us, with his spells and spies. There
> is no one to oppose him.
— sources/chapters/tss/tss-dunk-01.md:943 (edge G6, node brynden-rivers)

The same speech continues (not separately quoted here, to avoid re-duplicating the
adjacent "Mother marked Lord Rivers ... Bittersteel marked him ... Redgrass Field"
line already on `brynden-rivers.node.md`) that "Friends and favorites of Lord Rivers
fill every office, the lords of the small council lick his hand ... The Red Keep is
garrisoned by Raven's Teeth, and no man sees the king without his leave" — a
description of total, unaccountable control the "secretly a villain" strand leans on
directly.

**Melisandre's fire vision.** In R'hllor's flames, seeking Stannis, Melisandre
instead sees:

> A wooden face, corpse white. Was this the enemy? A thousand red eyes floated in the
> rising flames. He sees me. Beside him, a boy with a wolf’s face threw back his head
> and howled.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-melisandre-01.md:21 (edge G7, node melisandre)

Melisandre's own POV frames this as an open question ("Was this the enemy?"), not a
confirmed identification — but the pairing of a corpse-white wooden face (matching
Brynden's ADWD description) with "a boy with a wolf's face" (readable as Bran) inside
a fire vision that explicitly wonders whether it depicts "the enemy" is the theory's
strongest piece of outside corroboration that Brynden's nature reads as sinister to
at least one other magic-user in the text.

**Bran's own dread of becoming like Brynden.** Rescoped narrowly to what the text
actually shows (the stronger claim that Brynden "lured" Bran into danger and got his
friends killed is not re-argued here — it is already-established tier-1 plot, not a
new theory beat):

> He had thought the three-eyed crow would be a sorcerer, a wise old wizard who could
> fix his legs, but that was some stupid child’s dream, he realized now.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-03.md:121 (prose only — S216 fresh-verify DROPPED the proposed edge: Bran's disillusionment evidences how Brynden LOOKS, not a plan)

Bran's own disillusionment — expecting a benevolent healer-wizard and finding
instead a "grisly talking corpse" fused to a tree — is read by the theory as the
text's own quiet acknowledgment that whatever Brynden is, it is not what Bran (or the
reader) was led to hope for.

**"The bones of a thousand other dreamers."** Bran's very first crow-dream, long
before he ever reaches the cave:

> He saw the bones of a thousand other dreamers impaled upon their points. He was
> desperately afraid.
— sources/chapters/agot/agot-bran-03.md:101 (edge G9, node bran-stark)

The theory reads this image as evidence that Bran was not Brynden's only candidate —
that other "dreamers" were tried and discarded before him. (This is the same detail
the separate `eldritch-apocalypse` cluster's Euron-Bloodraven sub-claim leans on to
suggest Euron was once one of those rejected dreamers; that specific connection is
independently ungrounded in both clusters' source corpora — see Ungrounded material
below, and `eldritch-apocalypse.node.md`'s own adjudication.)

## Evidence Against

No book passage directly refutes either reading; the ambiguity is the theory's own
subject. The clearest textual counterweight is thematic rather than a citable beat
against Brynden specifically: elsewhere in the books, when Stannis considers killing
a child (Edric Storm) to further a war effort via blood magic, Davos's reply —
"Everything," to Stannis's own "what is the life of one bastard boy against a
kingdom?" — is offered by the source video as a values-check the "ends justify the
means" reading of Brynden should also have to answer. Neither Stannis's question nor
Davos's reply could be independently byte-verified against the 5-book corpus in this
pass (regrounding attempts returned no reliable match), so the parallel is not
represented as an edge — see Ungrounded material.

Within the grounded material itself, Brynden's own justification for his most
violent acts — ending a war by killing his own half-brother, arguing (per his own
words, already quoted on `brynden-rivers.node.md`) that he "sacrificed his own ...
honor for the good of the realm" — is at least as consistent with hard-nosed,
morally gray duty as with hidden villainy; the theory's own source video treats this
as genuinely unresolved rather than concluding against Brynden.

## Ungrounded material (outside the corpus)

- **community** — The claim that Brynden and Shiera Seastar were both personally
  fixated on "ancient scrolls" and "dusty prophecies" (beyond Aerys I's own
  established interest, which is on `aerys-ii-targaryen`-adjacent wiki material for a
  *different* Aerys) was not grounded to a specific passage this pass; the
  regrounding agent's attempted match (a description of Castle Black's library in a
  Jon POV chapter, unrelated to Aerys or Shiera) was a false positive and is
  explicitly excluded, not used as evidence.
- **community** — The "Azor Ahai bloodline breeding program" as a *century-long,
  Brynden-orchestrated* project (as opposed to the single grounded Aerys/Rhaella
  marriage, prose-only after the S216 drop) is the video's own explicitly hedged extension ("there's no
  knowing how deep this goes") and was not further grounded.
- **community (cross-cluster, avoid double-mint)** — "Bloodraven trained (and
  traumatized) Euron Greyjoy" is a full named sub-theory in the source video, resting
  on: Euron's self-mythologizing godhood claims and blood-magic trappings (his ship's
  red-painted deck, his shade-of-evening/warlock retinue — both real, well-grounded
  Euron passages: `sources/chapters/affc/affc-the-prophet-01.md:169` and
  `sources/chapters/affc/affc-the-reaver-01.md:259`); Euron's boast that as a boy he
  dreamed he could fly if he "leapt from some tall tower"; and the inference that
  Brynden, drawn to gifted dreamers, once visited young Euron the way he later
  reaches Bran. **The connecting claim itself — that Brynden ever contacted, trained,
  or traumatized Euron — has no textual anchor in this pass's corpus.** The two Euron
  quotes above establish only that Euron is villainous and magically dangerous, not
  that Brynden is involved; they are deliberately **not** represented as edges here,
  to avoid implying a connection the text doesn't support. This independently
  confirms the `eldritch-apocalypse` cluster's own S216 adjudication, which fenced
  the identical claim as "community-ungrounded" for the same reason. No double-mint:
  neither cluster edges this connection.
- **community (deferred future unit)** — "Quaithe is Shiera Seastar" is a named
  sub-theory (masked, magical, star-associated women; both need magic to have
  survived a century; the mask as concealment for Shiera's famous beauty). Of its
  five source beats, only Quaithe's plain in-text self-introduction — "I am Quaithe
  of the Shadow" (`sources/chapters/acok/acok-daenerys-01.md:211`) — byte-verified
  against the corpus, and it establishes only that Quaithe exists, not any identity
  claim; the identity argument itself (shared "star" motif, age, the mask-as-beauty-
  concealment reading) stayed ungrounded. `shiera-seastar.node.md`'s own Notes
  already flag "Fan theories connect her to Quaithe." Below the 3-edge bar for a
  standalone node this pass — held out as a future `quaithe-is-shiera-seastar` unit
  if a deeper pass grounds more of it, not minted here.
- **community** — Several further "villain" sub-claims from the source video could
  not be grounded to citable text this pass: that Brynden may want Bran's young body
  for himself; that he is secretly aiding the Others/Great Other; that the children
  of the forest's weirwood network may itself be a psychic hivemind manipulating him
  in turn; that Coldhands fed Bran human flesh (possibly Jojen's body); that Brynden
  edited Bran's memories of Jaime Lannister; and the show-only detail that Bran's
  powers leave him "robotic and dehumanized" per Meera's later complaint. All are the
  video's own speculation or show-derived, not grounded textual beats.
- **show** — Game of Thrones' later seasons depict a much-reduced version of the
  three-eyed raven's plan (Bran's training, no book-depicted resolution of whether
  Brynden is a villain) and drop the Euron/Bloodraven and Quaithe/Shiera threads
  entirely; none of this is book canon.
- **GRRM-craft-note** — The source video notes GRRM has written other stories
  featuring evil, seductive alien hiveminds as a possible authorial-parallel
  argument for the weirwood-network-as-hivemind reading. This is a craft observation
  about the author, not a claim about the books' plot, and is not reproduced as
  evidence.

## Status Notes

**Show:** the show's version of the three-eyed raven's plan for Bran is much thinner
than any of the book-side theories collected here, and never engages the "Brynden is
secretly a villain," "Bloodraven trained Euron," or "Quaithe is Shiera Seastar"
threads at all. `status: open` reflects that none of this unit's claim-strands have
any on-screen resolution, unlike `r-plus-l-equals-j`.

**ASX verdict:** the source video (1oEqnDAbCfE) presents the "grand plan" reading as
its central, fairly confident thread (strongly argued via the raven/Jon and
weirwood-merge evidence) while explicitly flagging the century-long breeding-program
extension as speculative. It treats "secretly a villain" as a genuine, unresolved
counter-reading of the *same* evidence rather than a separate claim to be ruled out,
concluding only that Brynden is "something more interesting" than a simple hero —
which is why this node holds both strands as facets of one theory rather than
splitting them into competing nodes.
