## Claim

The theory holds that the legend of the Night's King -- told to Bran Stark by Old
Nan, and recorded on the `nights-king` node -- is not merely backstory but a
pattern the books may be setting Jon Snow up to repeat. In the legend, the
thirteenth Lord Commander of the Night's Watch fell for a corpse-pale, blue-eyed
woman glimpsed from atop the Wall, made her his queen at the Nightfort, bound his
Sworn Brothers to his will with "strange sorceries," and -- it was discovered only
after his fall -- had been sacrificing to the Others throughout his reign, ending
(the theory reads) the first Long Night through a pact rather than a battle. The
theory extends this into two sub-claims: first, that the historical Long Night
itself may have ended this way, with the Night's Watch agreeing to hand over
children in a manner the text already shows repeating in the present day through
Craster's arrangement; second, that Jon -- installed as Lord Commander, allied with
wildlings, and surrounded by pale/blue-eyed imagery around the wildling woman Val
-- may face pressure or temptation (visionary, magical, or circumstantial) to
strike a similar bargain, becoming a new "Night's King" himself. The source video
(Alt Shift X, "The real Jon Snow") presents this explicitly as a dark temptation
Jon should refuse, not a predicted outcome -- see Status Notes.

Confidence stratification: the legend's own textual existence and content (as an
in-world tale Old Nan tells, and as a confirmed real-world precedent via Craster)
are solid, tier-3 premises; the extension onto Jon specifically -- that he
personally will face and be tempted by this choice -- has **no on-page grounding**
as of the end of *A Dance with Dragons* and rests entirely on community/GRRM-quote
speculation (see Ungrounded material). This is a thinner evidence base than the
wave-1 clusters: only one substrate beat survived byte-verification against the
book text.

## Evidence For

### The legend itself (subject-link)

The theory's entire premise is Old Nan's tale, recalled by Bran at the Nightfort
itself:

> He brought her back to the Nightfort and proclaimed her a queen and himself her
> king, and with strange sorceries he bound his Sworn Brothers to his will. For
> thirteen years they had ruled, Night's King and his corpse queen, till finally
> the Stark of Winterfell and Joramun of the wildlings had joined to free the
> Watch from bondage. After his fall, when it was found he had been sacrificing to
> the Others, all records of Night's King had been destroyed, his very name
> forbidden.
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-bran-04.md:101 (edge NK1, node nights-king)

This single passage carries the marriage-pact, the sacrifice-to-the-Others, and
the after-the-fact erasure that the Jon-parallel reading maps onto a possible
future.

### A rival telling: Night's King as a Stark

Old Nan's tale ends with competing guesses at the Night's King's true identity,
and she settles -- in her own telling -- on a Stark:

> He never was. He was a Stark, the brother of the man who brought him down.
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-bran-04.md:103 (edge NK2, node nights-king)

This variant is the textual basis for the theory's core Jon-parallel: a STARK
Lord Commander of the Night's Watch became the Night's King -- the precedent
pattern the theory speculates Jon (a Stark Lord Commander) could repeat (edge
NK2 carries exactly this reading, adjudicated S219). It does NOT ground the
separate "Starks possibly descended from that union" sub-claim, which remains
ungrounded speculation and lives only in this labelled section. Old Nan
herself immediately hedges it ("and who can say?"), and the wiki-sourced Recorded
History section already on this node's parent (`nights-king`) notes the identity
varies "depending on where the tale is told and who tells it."

### Craster's pact -- an on-page precedent

The theory's strongest surviving textual support is not about the legendary
Night's King at all, but about a living, present-day parallel: Craster, beyond
the Wall, trading his infant sons for safety. Jon puts the pattern into words to
Mormont:

> In half a hundred ways. "He gives his sons to the wood."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-jon-03.md:371 (edge NK3, node craster)

Mormont confirms it outright ("Yes," he says, twice) and later frames it
explicitly as an offering: "these boys are Craster's offerings. His prayers, if
you will." This is a confirmed, on-page instance of exactly the kind of
sacrifice-for-safety bargain the Night's King legend describes -- a precedent the
theory reads as making a future Jon-Others pact more plausible, even though
Craster's arrangement is his own, not the Watch's, and does not itself involve
Jon.

## Evidence Against

No on-page passage confirms or denies that Jon will face this choice -- the books
end (as of ADWD) with Jon freshly stabbed by his own brothers, before any such
temptation could be dramatized. The pushback that exists in the source material is
entirely outside the book text: the video's own verdict argues such a pact would
be moral corruption rather than real peace, and points out that both the Night's
King's and Craster's arrangements demonstrably failed to permanently stop the
Others -- but this reasoning is the video's commentary, not a quoted book passage,
and is represented as domain-labelled prose below rather than a CONTRADICTS edge.

## Status Notes

**Show:** Game of Thrones features a "Night King" as a named, singular
antagonist eventually killed by Arya Stark at the Battle of Winterfell -- but
this is a wholly separate show invention (the books have no confirmed singular
"Night King" villain; Melisandre attributes the white walkers to a nameless
"Great Other" instead, per the header's own sub_claim 3 note). The show never
depicted Jon becoming a new Night's King, marrying a corpse-queen figure, or
facing this specific temptation. This theory has **no show confirmation or
denial** -- status stays `open`.

**ASX verdict:** qSy2uaJ7ecU frames this explicitly as speculative and as a dark

it concludes such a pact would be moral corruption, not real peace, and that the
white walkers are a genuine, worthwhile threat to fight rather than appease.
