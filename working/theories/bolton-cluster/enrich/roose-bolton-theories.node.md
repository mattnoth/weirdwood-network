## Claim

The theory holds that "Roose Bolton" is not a single continuous man but a long-lived,
part-Other being — nicknamed "Bolt-On" by fans — who periodically discards an aging
or exposed identity and steals a new one by magically flaying and wearing another
person's skin, akin to (or an ancestor of) the Faceless Men's face-changing craft. A
first sub-claim holds this ability traces back to a Bolton ancestor's union with an
Other, as told in one of Old Nan's stories; a second holds Roose is himself
immortal or extremely long-lived and part-Other, possibly even the Night's King; a
third, most speculative extension holds Roose intends to eventually take his son
Ramsay's skin and identity as his next host, which is why he tolerates and
legitimizes Ramsay despite his erratic, embarrassing behavior.

## Evidence For

**Genre plausibility (scene-setting, not evidence specific to Roose).** ASOIAF's
setting already contains confirmed supernatural threats, which the theory leans on to
argue a skin-stealing immortal is not obviously less plausible than what the books
have already shown to be real:

> The cold winds are rising, and men go out from their fires and never come back … or
> if they do, they're not men no more, but only wights, with blue eyes and cold black
> hands.
— sources/chapters/agot/agot-bran-06.md:145 (prose only — deliberately NOT an edge;
Osha's speech to Bran confirms wights/Others exist in-world, but says nothing about
Roose specifically)

**A Bolton ancestor's union with an Other, per Old Nan.** The theory's origin point
for Bolton/Other blood is Old Nan's tale of the Night's King, told to Bran:

> A woman was his downfall; a woman glimpsed from atop the Wall, with skin as white
> as the moon and eyes like blue stars. Fearing nothing, he chased her and caught
> her and loved her, though her skin was cold as ice, and when he gave his seed to
> her he gave his soul as well.
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-bran-04.md:99 (edge B1, node old-nan)

The woman is broadly read (by the theory and by fandom generally) as an Other or
Other-adjacent being; the human king "gives his seed" to her, which the theory reads
as implying an heir could have been born of the union.

**Roose's own words link leeching to long life.** The single most direct piece of
textual evidence — Roose explaining his leeching habit to Arya-as-Nan at Harrenhal:

> "My squire could take a lesson from you, it would seem. Frequent leechings are the
> secret of a long life. A man must purge himself of bad blood."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-arya-09.md:385 (edge B3, node roose-bolton)

The theory reads this as more than idiosyncratic medical superstition — a literal
technique for extending an unnaturally long life. A separate passage, Reek's POV
outside the Winterfell wedding tent, independently describes Roose in terms the
theory reads as consistent with an unnaturally preserved body: "Though well past
forty, he was as yet unwrinkled, with scarce a line to tell of the passage of time.
… There was an agelessness about him, a stillness; on Roose Bolton's face, rage and
joy looked much the same." (sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-reek-02.md:221 — prose only,
not a separate edge, to avoid a third citation drawn from `roose-bolton` itself.)

**Ramsay's eyes already match Roose's.** The theory reads this as functionally
useful *if* a body-swap were ever to happen — no one would notice a sudden change in
Ramsay's eye color:

> He had his lord father's eyes—small, close-set, queerly pale. Ghost grey, some men
> called the shade, but in truth his eyes were all but colorless, like two chips of
> dirty ice.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-reek-01.md:91 (edge B5, node ramsay-snow)

**A mechanism borrowed from an unrelated in-text detail.** Coldhands' explanation of
what happens to a corpse's blood after death is used by the theory to justify why an
undead-or-Other-adjacent Roose would need to leech at all:

> "Once the heart has ceased to beat, a man's blood runs down into his extremities,
> where it thickens and congeals." … "His hands and feet swell up and turn as black
> as pudding."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-bran-01.md:201 (prose only — S216 fresh-verify
adjudication DROPPED this as an edge: the passage describes wight physiology beyond
the Wall and never mentions Roose; the theory's use of it is an imported analogy)

This is the weakest chain in the theory's own logic: Coldhands is describing wight
physiology beyond the Wall, and nothing in the text connects that mechanism to Roose
Bolton at all — the theory imports it as an explanatory analogy, not a stated fact
about him.

**Roose's own account of Domeric's death reads, per the theory, as evasive.** Roose
tells Theon that he has not killed Ramsay for (probably) murdering his trueborn heir
Domeric only because it would be kinslaying:

> "Now his bones lie beneath the Dreadfort with the bones of his brothers, who died
> still in the cradle, and I am left with Ramsay. Tell me, my lord … if the
> kinslayer is accursed, what is a father to do when one son slays another?"
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-reek-03.md:251 (edge B7, node domeric-bolton; Roose
explains, moments earlier in the same speech, that he believes Ramsay poisoned
Domeric — "Ramsay killed him. A sickness of the bowels, Maester Uthor says, but I
say poison" — before posing this rhetorical question)

The theory (and the source video) questions whether a man who breaks guest right to
massacre his own bannermen and allies at the Red Wedding would really be stayed by a
kinslaying taboo, and reads the rhetorical question as covering for an unstated
reason Roose tolerates Ramsay.

**House Bolton's flaying culture, as a thematic (not magical) parallel.** The theory
notes the Boltons already have a real-world cultural practice of removing skin,
which it treats as suggestive scaffolding for the idea that a magical version of
skin-removal could exist in the same family:

> She had served as Roose Bolton's cupbearer at Harrenhal, and he would flay you if
> you spilled his wine.
— sources/chapters/affc/affc-arya-02.md:247 (prose only — S216 adjudication DROPPED
this as an edge per the premise-prose precedent: thematic scaffolding, not evidence)

This is presented at face value as literal, non-magical punitive flaying — the
theory's leap from "the Boltons flay people" to "the Boltons can magically wear
people's skins" is not supported by anything in this passage itself.

## Evidence Against

**Old Nan explicitly rules out "a Bolton" as the answer, in the very same story the
theory builds from.** The line most often cited to link House Bolton to the Night's
King is immediately and explicitly contradicted within the same passage:

> "Some say he was a Bolton," Old Nan would always end. "Some say a Magnar out of
> Skagos, some say Umber, Flint, or Norrey. … He never was. He was a Stark, the
> brother of the man who brought him down."
— sources/chapters/asos/asos-bran-04.md:103 (edge B2, node old-nan, CONTRADICTS)

This directly undercuts the theory's specific "Bolton ancestor" framing of the
Night's King story: in-world, "he was a Bolton" is presented as one of several wrong
folk guesses, and Old Nan's own punchline names the Night's King a Stark instead.
The union-with-an-Other story itself (edge B1) still stands as a real textual event
in the tale — it simply is not, per the text's own resolution, a Bolton.

**The "pale grey mask" line is set inside an atmospheric, all-guests-affected
description, not a Roose-specific revelation.** The theory's other favorite physical
description occurs in a passage that explicitly frames every wedding guest's face as
monstrously distorted by torchlight and mist, not just Roose's:

> The way the mists threw back the shifting light made their features seem bestial,
> half-human, twisted. Lord Stout became a mastiff, old Lord Locke a vulture,
> Whoresbane Umber a gargoyle, Big Walder Frey a fox, Little Walder a red bull …
> Roose Bolton's own face was a pale grey mask, with two chips of dirty ice where his
> eyes should be.
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-the-prince-of-winterfell-01.md:61 (prose only — S216
fresh-verify REJECTED the proposed SUPPORTS edge here as cherry-picked; the passage
stays as the counter-reading it documents)

Read in context, "pale grey mask" sits in a list of *every* guest's face being
warped by the same lighting trick (a mastiff, a vulture, a gargoyle, a fox, a red
bull) — the theory treats Roose's entry as literal while treating the others as
figurative, with nothing in the text singling his out.

## Ungrounded material (outside the corpus)

- **show** — Game of Thrones depicts Roose Bolton dying an entirely mundane death:
  stabbed by his own son Ramsay in Season 6, with no skin-change, body-swap, or any
  supernatural element shown or implied. This is a show-only outcome (the books have
  not depicted Roose's death), but as a data point it cuts *against* the theory
  rather than confirming it — the opposite of how show outcomes function for
  `r-plus-l-equals-j`. Status stays `open`, never `show-confirmed`.
- **show** — the video's aside that the show separately establishes magical
  "fireball-throwing" children of the forest is flagged by the video's own narrator
  as show material, offered only as further genre-normalization for supernatural
  elements generally; it says nothing about Roose and was not grounded to any book
  passage.
- **community** — The mechanism itself (periodic body-swapping specifically timed to
  Ramsay's legitimization as heir of the Dreadfort/North) is the theory's central
  speculative claim and has no citable textual anchor; it is inference layered on
  top of the grounded quotes above, not a beat in its own right.
- **community** — The alternative reading that Ramsay's matching pale eyes indicate
  Ramsay himself has some Other-ness (rather than being explained by ordinary
  paternity) is unattributed speculation with no textual anchor.
- **community** — The suggestion that Roose (not Ramsay) personally murdered Domeric,
  and/or murdered other trueborn children of his for lacking pale eyes, has no
  textual support beyond the ambiguous kinslaying rhetoric already captured in edge
  B7; the video itself frames both as speculative extensions.
- **community** — The claim that Roose is specifically part-Other or literally the
  Night's King himself (rather than merely descended from some other unspecified
  union) is the theory's most speculative extension and was not grounded to any
  additional passage beyond edges B1/B2.
- **community** — A thematic objection recorded by the source video itself: ASOIAF is
  generally about complex human beings doing wrong out of love, belonging,
  brokenness, and psychosis rather than good-vs-monster stories, so recasting
  Roose's cruelty as literal monstrousness risks cheapening that theme. The video's
  own rebuttal — that literal monsters (the Others) already coexist with that theme
  elsewhere in the story — is genre-level reasoning, not a textual beat about Roose.
