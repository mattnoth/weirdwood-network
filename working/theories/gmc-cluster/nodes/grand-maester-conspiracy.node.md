## Claim

The theory holds that the maesters of the Citadel are not the neutral, apolitical
healers and scholars they present themselves as, but a secret, coordinated order
working against House Targaryen, dragons, and magic across generations. It chains
together three sub-claims voiced by two named in-world proponents: (1) a maester
named Walys — reportedly the bastard son of a Hightower girl and a Citadel
archmaester — was involved in a plot with the Starks, Tullys, and possibly the
Arryns and Baratheons that helped set Robert's Rebellion in motion, per Lady Barbrey
Dustin; (2) the maesters killed off the Targaryen dragons, directly or indirectly —
whether by manipulating House Hightower into starting the Dance of the Dragons or by
poisoning or stunting the last hatchlings — per Marwyn the Mage; and (3) the maesters
are conspiring to build a world without sorcery, prophecy, glass candles, or dragons:
a world without magic, also per Marwyn. The theory treats these as evidence of one
underlying institutional agenda rather than three unrelated grievances, though (as
the Evidence Against section documents) the text supplies a real, individually
grounded counter-motive for nearly every maester the theory implicates.

## Evidence For

**Barbrey Dustin's case against the "grey rats."** Lady Dustin, addressing Theon
directly, lays out the general mechanism by which she believes the maesters exert
hidden power — access, secrecy, and dependency:

> "If I were queen, the first thing I would do would be to kill all those grey rats.
> They scurry everywhere, living on the leavings of the lords, chittering to one
> another, whispering in the ears of their masters."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-the-prince-of-winterfell-01.md:161 (edge M1, node
barbrey-dustin)

In the same conversation she goes further — that maesters shed their birth names on
taking the chain specifically so no one can trace their loyalties, and that lords
who lean on them for comfort in grief and illness end up ruled by them rather than
the reverse: "before too long, the ruler has become the ruled"
(adwd-the-prince-of-winterfell-01.md:165) — a companion passage the theory reads as
the general thesis Walys's specific case (below) is meant to illustrate.

**Walys Flowers and the road to Robert's Rebellion.** Dustin names a specific agent
and a specific plot: Maester Walys, sent to Winterfell under the cover of his
maester's anonymity, was secretly a Hightower's son by a Citadel archmaester, and
used his position to steer Lord Rickard Stark toward the Tully marriage that (per
the theory) helped set the alliance of Robert's Rebellion in motion:

> "Walys Flowers had a Hightower girl for a mother … and an archmaester of the
> Citadel for a father, it was rumored. ... Once he forged his chain, his secret
> father and his friends wasted no time dispatching him to Winterfell to fill Lord
> Rickard's ears with poisoned words as sweet as honey. The Tully marriage was his
> notion, never doubt it."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-the-prince-of-winterfell-01.md:167 (edge M2, node
walys-flowers)

This is Dustin's own allegation, not narration-confirmed fact — the walys-flowers
node's own `## Origins` records only the neutral biographical facts (his parentage,
his posting to Winterfell); the claim that he orchestrated the Tully marriage lives
only in this quote, which is why it stays available as theory evidence rather than
premise.

**Marwyn's two core claims.** Confronting Samwell Tarly at the end of *A Feast for
Crows*, Archmaester Marwyn makes both of the theory's sharpest, most direct
assertions in a single breath. On the dragons:

> "Who do you think killed all the dragons the last time around? Gallant
> dragonslayers armed with swords?"
— sources/chapters/affc/affc-samwell-05.md:217 (edge M3, node marwyn)

And on magic generally:

> "The world the Citadel is building has no place in it for sorcery or prophecy or
> glass candles, much less for dragons."
— sources/chapters/affc/affc-samwell-05.md:217 (edge M3 — S216 adjudication merged the former M4 into M3: same Marwyn sentence, co-dependent testimony, one edge)

The same speech supplies the theory's account of *why* Aemon Targaryen was denied
his own archmaester's chain and sent to the Wall instead ("His blood was why. He
could not be trusted. No more than I can.") and Marwyn's own admission that the
Citadel is willing to kill to keep this quiet ("If I tell you, they may need to
kill you too.") — both companion lines in the same passage, cited as prose rather
than a third edge from the identical quote block. Marwyn's credibility on this
front is bolstered by his own unconventional profile: he is the Citadel's other
outlier on magic, wears a Valyrian steel ring/rod/mask marking him the order's
resident expert on it, and studied for years among warlocks and shadowbinders in
the east (affc-samwell-05.md:189).

**Pycelle's role in the Sack of King's Landing.** Independent of Dustin's and
Marwyn's testimony, a third maester's own confession under interrogation
corroborates the broader pattern of a maester acting decisively against a
Targaryen king. Cornered by Cersei and Tyrion, Pycelle admits:

> "’twas I who bid Aerys open his gates . . ."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-tyrion-06.md:291 (edge M5, node pycelle)

Pycelle's advice to Aerys II to open the gates to Tywin Lannister's "loyal" army
directly enabled the Sack of King's Landing, the death of the Mad King, and the
end of Targaryen rule in Westeros — a maester's hand on the very last domino of the
dynasty's fall, regardless of what is later revealed about his motive (see Evidence
Against).

**Cressen's attempt on "the red witch."** The theory's third case study is Maester
Cressen at Dragonstone, who is shown taking concrete, premeditated action against
a practitioner of magic. He carries a fatal poison specifically to use against
Melisandre:

> "It was made from a certain plant that grew only on the islands of the Jade Sea
> ... the potion must be thickened with ash and allowed to crystallize."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-prologue.md:269 (edge M6, node cressen)

He frames Melisandre in his own thoughts as a contagion to be stopped: "Melisandre,
whose madness must not be allowed to spread beyond Dragonstone"
(acok-prologue.md:265) — read by the theory as a maester independently enacting the
order's anti-magic agenda, unprompted by any council or instruction from Oldtown.

**Magic returning independent of maester denial.** Quaithe's warning to Daenerys —
delivered the same year Marwyn reaches the identical conclusion from his own glass
candle — is offered as proof the "no magic" world the maesters have built is
already crumbling around them:

> "Hear me, Daenerys Targaryen. The glass candles are burning."
— sources/chapters/adwd/adwd-daenerys-02.md:139 (edge M7, node quaithe, tier-4)

The theory reads this less as evidence of the *conspiracy itself* than as evidence
of its stakes: if the Citadel's project really is to suppress magic, the glass
candles lighting again — independently observed by both a mysterious shadowbinder
and the Citadel's own resident magic-expert — mean that project is failing.

## Evidence Against

**Pycelle's own confession undercuts the anti-Targaryen reading of his own act.**
The same interrogation that yields Pycelle's admission about opening the gates also
yields his real motive, and it is not ideological:

> "All I did, I did for House Lannister."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-tyrion-06.md:291 (edge M8, node pycelle,
CONTRADICTS, tier-3)

Pycelle goes on, sobbing, to describe decades of personal loyalty to Tywin
Lannister specifically — not to any Citadel-wide anti-Targaryen program. This does
not erase the *effect* of his advice on House Targaryen (edge M5 stands on its own
facts), but it directly contradicts reading that advice as evidence of an
*organized maester conspiracy* rather than one old man's personal devotion to a
single great lord.

**Cressen's own motive is explicitly fatherly, not doctrinal.** The prologue that
supplies the theory's cleanest "maester moves against magic" case study spends far
more time establishing a different motive altogether. Cressen has raised Stannis,
Robert, and Renly like sons since their father's death, and his interior monologue
is unambiguous about why he wants Melisandre gone:

> "When a maester donned his collar, he put aside the hope of children, yet Cressen
> had oft felt a father nonetheless. Robert, Stannis, Renly . . . three sons he had
> raised after the angry sea claimed Lord Steffon. ... He could not allow it, would
> not allow it."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-prologue.md:263 (edge M9, node cressen, CONTRADICTS)

He is horrified specifically by the prospect of Stannis killing his own brother
Renly ("Fratricide . . . my lord, this is evil, unthinkable," acok-prologue.md:253)
— a personal, protective motive the text foregrounds well ahead of any general
maester ideology about magic. This is the same tension the ASX video itself
highlights in using Cressen and Pycelle as test cases: both maesters' actions are
individually consistent with the theory's broad strokes, but the text supplies each
of them with a fully sufficient personal motive that makes an organized ideological
conspiracy unnecessary to explain what they did.

**Maester Luwin states the Citadel's actual official position on magic, and it is
skepticism, not secret suppression.** If the maesters are running a coordinated
campaign against something they consider a real and present threat, Luwin's own
words to Bran describe an order that mostly does not believe magic is real at all:

> "Perhaps magic was once a mighty force in the world, but no longer. What little
> remains is no more than the wisp of smoke that lingers in the air after a great
> fire has burned out, and even that is fading."
— sources/chapters/acok/acok-bran-04.md:223 (edge M10, node luwin, CONTRADICTS,
tier-3)

An order of genuine skeptics has less need of an active suppression campaign than
one that secretly knows magic works and fears it — though the theory can (and the
source video does) read this skepticism itself as the party line the Citadel wants
believed, which is why this stays a contradiction of degree rather than a knockout.

**The Doom of Valyria, not the Citadel, is where the text says magic actually
died.** Daenerys's own understanding of magic's history — set centuries before any
maester conspiracy could plausibly have organized itself around the Targaryens
specifically — locates magic's original decline at a catastrophe with no maester
involvement at all:

> "Magic had died in the west when the Doom fell on Valyria and the Lands of the
> Long Summer, and neither spell-forged steel nor stormsingers nor dragons could
> hold it back."
— sources/chapters/agot/agot-daenerys-03.md:157 (edge M11, node
daenerys-targaryen, CONTRADICTS, tier-4)

This complicates only the "maesters caused magic's decline" strand of sub-claim 3,
not the narrower claim that maesters are actively hostile to whatever magic
remains.

## Ungrounded material (outside the corpus)

- **twoiaf** — The theory's motive-building argument (that the maesters, as
  Westeros's historians, would be uniquely aware of the damage done by Targaryen
  war, misrule, and madness, and that several Grand Maesters were killed by
  Targaryen kings) is drawn from *The World of Ice and Fire* rather than any
  passage in the five-book POV corpus.
- **twoiaf** — The counter-argument that Aemon went to the Wall specifically so he
  could not be used politically against his brother King Aegon V ("Egg") — rather
  than because the Citadel distrusted him as a Targaryen — comes from the World of
  Ice and Fire companion app, not the books. Likewise the claim that at least one
  Targaryen has served as archmaester historically.
- **fab** — The detailed count of how the last 21 Targaryen dragons died during the
  Dance of the Dragons (12 in battle, 5 to a rioting mob, 2 flown off, 1 vanished),
  and the claim that Grand Maesters Orwyle and Runciter both tried to *prevent* the
  Dance of the Dragons from starting, come from *Fire & Blood*/the Dance of the
  Dragons novellas, outside the five-book corpus this cluster is grounded against.
  The related claim that a non-maester septon and court fool (Mushroom) also
  contributed to the historical record of the Dance — offered by the video as a
  reason a maester cover-up of that war looks less likely — is real *Fire & Blood*
  text but was judged too indirect (evidence about the reliability of historians in
  general, not about the maester conspiracy itself) to mint as an edge; it is
  cross-referenced here by name (nodes `mushroom`, `runciter`) rather than quoted.
- **fab** — House Hightower's role in starting the Dance of the Dragons (Queen
  Alicent favoring her son Aegon over Viserys's chosen heir Rhaenyra) is real
  *Fire & Blood* content, and the theory notes House Hightower is the same house
  linked to Walys via Dustin's account — but whether the Hightowers were themselves
  manipulated by the Citadel, or acted on their own ambition, is not stated
  anywhere in the corpus; the counter-observation that the Hightowers have
  otherwise been conspicuously loyal to House Targaryen is likewise *Fire & Blood*
  background, not POV-book text.
- **community** — That the stunted, small size of Daenerys's dragons' predecessors
  (the last dragons before her own) might indicate deliberate poisoning is fan
  speculation with no cited textual source.
- **community** — The suggestion that Pycelle's doddering-old-man act could itself
  be a deception concealing a deeper conspiratorial role, and that Marwyn's own
  claims might be exaggerated or self-serving (to keep Sam from reporting to the
  Citadel about Daenerys), are both unfalsifiable readings the video itself raises
  and does not resolve.
- **book-interpretive** — That the maesters manipulated House Hightower into
  starting the Dance of the Dragons "in the same way" they are alleged to have
  manipulated the Starks into Robert's Rebellion is the video's own explicit
  extrapolation by analogy, not a textually stated parallel.
- **book-interpretive** — The convergence of several outside figures on the
  Citadel late in the published books — Sam sent to study, Jaqen H'ghar arriving in
  disguise with a key said to open every door, Sarella Martell posing as the
  acolyte Alleras, and Euron Greyjoy's ships raiding toward Oldtown — is real,
  independently verifiable plot setup (affc-samwell-05.md:175 and surrounding
  text), but is itself only a structural observation that "more will be revealed,"
  not evidence of any conspiracy's content; kept out of the edge set as
  atmosphere/setup rather than evidence.

## Status Notes

**Show outcome:** none. *Game of Thrones* gave the Citadel and its maesters a
minor, mostly comic role (Qyburn's arc diverges into his own separate plotline)
and never depicted or addressed an organized anti-Targaryen or anti-magic maester
conspiracy in any form. `status` stays `open` — book-only material, neither
confirmed nor jossed.

**ASX verdict:** the video's own conclusion is deliberately hedged: no hard
evidence of an organized, realm-wide conspiracy exists, but it is "very believable"
that at least some individual archmaesters distrust Targaryens, dragons, and magic,
and might occasionally act on that distrust — "there's probably some sneaky shit
going on, we just don't know how much." The video treats Pycelle and Cressen
explicitly as test cases and concludes both are better explained by personal motive
than by ideology, while treating Marwyn's testimony (delivered by a Citadel insider
with nothing obvious to gain from lying to Sam) as the strongest single piece of
evidence for sub-claims 2 and 3.
