## Identity

A hedge knight is an itinerant, unaffiliated knight in Westeros — one who holds no lord's retainer fee and owes sworn service to no house. The name derives from sleeping rough beneath hedges while traveling between tourneys and odd work. Hedge knights are formally knighted and bear the title "Ser," but they occupy the lowest rung of knightly society: without lands, stipend, or a fixed seat, they sustain themselves through tourney prizes, mercenary contracts, and occasional short-term service arrangements with lords willing to hire them. The lifestyle is associated with poverty, social marginality, and a precarious honor — Kyle the Cat's node captures this well: he "longed for the luxuries and comforts that were difficult for a hedge knight of his age to attain, and was willing to compromise his honor to get them." A cynical summary from Ser Lucas Inchfield's node describes them as "beggars with blades at best, outlaws at worst."

Hedge knights congregate around tourneys, which offer both prize money and the chance to attract a lord's notice. The Whitewalls tourney in 212 AC drew a camp of them near the Gods Eye — Ser Kyle the Cat, Ser Maynard Plumm, Ser Glendon Ball, and Ser Duncan the Tall among them — sheltering together in a ring of weirwood stumps, sharing meals and gossip while angling for permanent placement. Duncan's node shows that after the Trial of Seven at Ashford, he explicitly chose to remain a hedge knight despite being offered a fixed post at Summerhall, believing the wandering life would teach his royal squire Egg humility.

Socially, the rank reads as barely above baseborn. Petyr Baelish's node records a contemporary dismissal that Littlefinger was "of such undistinguished birth, one step up from a hedge knight." The Frey reaction to Amerei's forced marriage to Pate of the Blue Fork underscores the stigma: the family considered marrying their daughter to "a bloody hedge knight" a humiliating outcome. Yet the title remains distinct from common sellswords or outlaws — hedge knights are proper knights, bound in theory by knightly vows, and their tourney participation is legitimate. Ser Uthor Underleaf, described as a hedge knight who "preferred not to be called as such," drew a distinction for himself: "I am a tourney knight, the best that you are ever like to meet." Clayton Suggs's node notes he was "an upjumped hedge knight, as crude as he was strong" — the phrasing "upjumped" confirming the status improvement implied by his later rise.
