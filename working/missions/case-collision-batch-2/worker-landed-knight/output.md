## Identity

A landed knight is a rank in the social hierarchy of Westeros, sitting below lords but above landless knights (hedge knights). Unlike hedge knights, who wander and take service where they can, a landed knight holds a small parcel of land — typically a modest tower, holdfast, or hamlet — and may owe direct feudal obligations to a lord or house rather than a great lord.

The distinction carries social weight: a landed knight is considered a respectable but decidedly minor ranking. In one passage, Ser Barristan Selmy reflects that a landed knight "is no fit consort for a princess of royal blood," illustrating the gulf between this station and the nobility. Conversely, the rank can represent a demotion from lordship: House Connington, formerly lords, was reduced to landed knights as a consequence of Robert's Rebellion, with Ser Ronnet Connington explicitly described as "a landed knight, no more." Similarly, Bonifer Hasty is described as a landed knight in contrast to the higher status expected for a suitor to Rhaella Targaryen.

The term also appears in the context of the Dance of the Dragons, indicating the rank was well established across Westerosi history. Landed knights typically hold specific castles or seats tied to their title (e.g., the Knight of Griffin's Roost), distinguishing them from hedge knights who hold nothing.
